# positive-psychology

Positive psychology (Seligman & Csikszentmihalyi, 2000) studies what makes life worth living — strengths, positive emotions, engagement, meaning, and accomplishment. It is not "positive thinking" or ignoring problems — it is an evidence-based approach to building wellbeing alongside (not instead of) addressing psychopathology. Meta-analyses show positive psychology interventions significantly increase wellbeing and decrease depressive symptoms (Bolier et al., 2013; Sin & Lyubomirsky, 2009).

## Character strengths (VIA — Peterson & Seligman, 2004)

The VIA (Values in Action) classification identifies 24 character strengths organized under 6 virtues. Unlike deficit-focused assessment, VIA identifies what is right with a person.

### The 24 strengths

| Virtue | Strengths |
|---|---|
| **Wisdom** | Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment/Critical thinking, Love of learning, Perspective/Wisdom |
| **Courage** | Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty/Authenticity, Zest/Vitality |
| **Humanity** | Love, Kindness, Social intelligence |
| **Justice** | Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership |
| **Temperance** | Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-regulation |
| **Transcendence** | Appreciation of beauty & excellence, Gratitude, Hope/Optimism, Humor, Spirituality |

### Assessment

- **VIA Survey:** Free at viacharacter.org — 96 items, ~15 minutes, identifies rank order of all 24 strengths
- **Signature strengths:** Top 5 strengths that feel most natural, energizing, and essential to who you are

### Strengths-based exercises

**Use a signature strength in a new way (Seligman et al., 2005):**
- Identify top 5 strengths from VIA survey
- Each day for one week, use one signature strength in a new and different way
- Example: if Curiosity is a signature strength → explore a new neighborhood, ask a colleague about their culture, read about an unfamiliar topic
- Evidence: increased happiness and decreased depression at 6-month follow-up

**Strengths spotting:**
- Notice when strengths appear naturally in daily life
- Ask: "What strength was I using when I felt most engaged/alive today?"
- Help clients identify strengths they don't recognize in themselves

**Strengths-based goal setting:**
- Align goals with top strengths for intrinsic motivation
- "How can you use your strength of [X] to approach this challenge?"
- Goals aligned with strengths feel more natural and sustainable

## Gratitude interventions

### Three good things (Seligman et al., 2005)

One of the most researched and robustly effective positive psychology interventions.

**Instructions:**
1. Each evening, write down 3 good things that happened today
2. For each, answer: "Why did this good thing happen?"
3. Things can be small ("a good cup of coffee") or large ("got the promotion")
4. Duration: minimum 1 week; many people continue indefinitely

**Evidence:** Increased happiness and decreased depression at 1, 3, and 6 months. Effect strengthens with continued practice.

**Variations:**
- Three good things about yourself (for low self-esteem)
- Three good things in a relationship (for couples)
- Family gratitude at dinner (for families with children)

### Gratitude letter/visit (Seligman et al., 2005)

Powerful single-session intervention with immediate and lasting effects.

**Instructions:**
1. Think of someone who made a significant positive difference in your life whom you never properly thanked
2. Write a detailed letter (300+ words): what they did, how it affected your life, what it meant to you
3. Arrange a visit and read the letter to them in person
4. If in-person is impossible: video call, or send the letter

**Evidence:** Largest immediate happiness boost of any positive intervention. Effects visible at 1-month follow-up.

### Gratitude journal

- Daily or weekly reflection on things you appreciate
- More flexible than "three good things" — write freely
- Avoid gratitude fatigue: vary what you write about, don't force it

### Mental subtraction (Koo et al., 2008)

- Imagine your life without a positive event or person
- "What if I had never met [partner]? What if I hadn't gotten into [university]?"
- Counterintuitively, imagining absence increases appreciation more than counting blessings

## Flow (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990)

Flow is a state of complete absorption in an activity where time seems to stop and self-consciousness disappears. Associated with peak performance and deep satisfaction.

### Conditions for flow

1. **Clear goals:** You know what you're trying to do at each moment
2. **Immediate feedback:** You know how well you're doing
3. **Challenge-skill balance:** The challenge slightly exceeds your current skill level

### The 8 dimensions

1. Clear goals
2. Concentration and focus
3. Loss of self-consciousness
4. Distorted sense of time (usually faster)
5. Direct and immediate feedback
6. Balance between challenge and skill
7. Sense of personal control
8. Intrinsically rewarding (autotelic)

### Flow-activity identification

Help clients discover their flow activities:
- "When do you lose track of time?"
- "What activities make you feel fully engaged and energized afterward (not drained)?"
- "When do you feel most like yourself?"

### Flow diary

Track flow experiences for 1-2 weeks:

| Date | Activity | Duration | Flow level (1-10) | What enabled it? | What disrupted it? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | | |

### Increasing flow in daily life

- Increase challenge in routine activities (mastery)
- Reduce distractions during absorbing activities
- Set clear micro-goals for tasks
- Choose activities that match current skill + slight stretch

## Savoring (Bryant & Veroff, 2007)

Savoring is the capacity to attend to, appreciate, and enhance positive experiences. The positive counterpart to coping (managing negative experiences).

### Three temporal orientations

| Orientation | Focus | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Reminiscence** | Savoring the past | Looking at old photos with warm appreciation |
| **Mindful absorption** | Savoring the present | Fully tasting each bite of a wonderful meal |
| **Anticipation** | Savoring the future | Excited planning of an upcoming trip |

### Savoring strategies

- **Sharing with others:** Telling someone about a positive experience amplifies it
- **Memory building:** Taking photos, journaling, creating souvenirs
- **Self-congratulation:** Acknowledging your role in making something good happen
- **Sharpening perception:** Focusing on specific sensory details
- **Absorption:** Letting yourself be fully immersed without thinking about it

### Savoring exercises

**Savoring walk (20 min):**
- Walk slowly through a pleasant area
- Notice beautiful things — light, colors, textures, sounds, smells
- Take "mental photographs" of moments that catch your attention
- Share the experience with someone afterward

**Savoring a meal:**
- Eat one meal slowly and with full attention
- Notice flavors, textures, temperatures, aromas
- Put utensils down between bites
- Express gratitude for the food

## Best possible self (King, 2001)

### Instructions

1. Set a timer for 20 minutes
2. Write about your life in 5-10 years, imagining everything has gone as well as possible
3. You have worked hard and achieved all your life goals
4. Write in vivid, concrete detail: What does your day look like? Where do you live? Who is around you? What are you doing for work? How do you feel?
5. Don't worry about realism — this is about your deepest aspirations

**Repeat:** Daily or weekly for 4+ weeks.

**Evidence:** Increases optimism, positive affect, and wellbeing (Meevissen et al., 2011). Also improves goal pursuit by clarifying values.

**Therapeutic use:**
- Clarifies what the client truly values (vs. what they think they "should" want)
- Reveals discrepancies between current life and aspirations
- Motivates change by making the desired future vivid
- Can be combined with values work from ACT

## Meaning-making

### Ikigai exploration

Ikigai (Japanese concept) — the intersection of:
1. What you **love** (passion)
2. What you're **good at** (vocation)
3. What the **world needs** (mission)
4. What you can be **paid for** (profession)

**Exercise:** Draw four overlapping circles. Brainstorm items in each circle. Look for overlaps. What lives at the center?

### Legacy project

- "What do you want to leave behind?"
- "What matters most to you — what would you fight for?"
- "If you could teach one thing to the next generation, what would it be?"

Connects to purpose and meaning beyond daily concerns.

### Narrative identity (McAdams)

The story you tell yourself about who you are — your personal narrative. It can be reauthored.

- **Redemption sequences:** Stories where bad events lead to good outcomes ("It was terrible, but I grew from it")
- **Contamination sequences:** Stories where good events are ruined ("It was great, but then...")
- Redemption narratives are associated with better wellbeing
- Therapy can help shift contamination sequences toward redemption without denying the pain

### Benefit finding

Identifying growth or positive meaning in difficult experiences. Not minimizing suffering — finding what coexists with it.

- "Is there anything you've gained or learned through this difficulty?"
- "Has this experience changed you in any ways that you value?"
- Timing matters: too early feels dismissive. Wait until the client is ready.

## Resilience building

### ABC model of resilience (Reivich & Shatté, 2002)

- **A** = Adversity (the event)
- **B** = Beliefs (what you tell yourself about it)
- **C** = Consequences (emotions and behaviors that follow)

Change B to change C. Identify catastrophic beliefs, challenge them, generate alternatives.

### Challenge vs threat appraisal

The same event can be appraised as:
- **Challenge:** "This is hard but I can handle it, and I'll grow" → approach, engagement, performance
- **Threat:** "This is dangerous and I might fail" → avoidance, anxiety, impaired performance

**Reframing exercise:** "How could this be a challenge rather than a threat? What's the growth opportunity?"

### Post-traumatic growth (Tedeschi & Calhoun, 1996)

Growth that can emerge from struggle with highly challenging life crises. Not inevitable, not universal, not a silver lining — but real and documented.

**Five domains:**
1. **New possibilities:** "I discovered new paths in life"
2. **Relating to others:** "I have deeper, more meaningful relationships"
3. **Personal strength:** "I know I can handle difficult things"
4. **Appreciation of life:** "I value each day more"
5. **Spiritual/existential change:** "I have a deeper understanding of life's meaning"

**Post-Traumatic Growth Inventory (PTGI):** 21-item self-report measure. Useful for tracking growth over time.

### Coping repertoire

Building a diverse toolkit of strategies rather than relying on one approach:
- Problem-focused coping (when you can change the situation)
- Emotion-focused coping (when you can't change the situation)
- Meaning-focused coping (finding purpose in adversity)
- Social coping (seeking support)
- Physical coping (exercise, sleep, nutrition)

## PERMA model (Seligman, 2011)

Five pillars of flourishing. Each can be independently cultivated.

### P — Positive Emotion

Frequency of pleasant feelings: joy, gratitude, serenity, interest, hope, pride, amusement, inspiration, awe, love.

**Assessment:** "On a scale of 1-10, how often do you experience positive emotions in a typical week?"
**Interventions:** Three good things, savoring, pleasant activity scheduling, gratitude practices.

### E — Engagement

Using signature strengths to achieve flow states.

**Assessment:** "How often do you feel completely absorbed in what you're doing?"
**Interventions:** Flow diary, strengths-based activity scheduling, challenge-skill optimization.

### R — Relationships

Positive, supportive connections with others.

**Assessment:** "How satisfied are you with your close relationships? Do you feel connected to others?"
**Interventions:** Active constructive responding, gratitude visits, quality time scheduling, social strengths use.

### M — Meaning

Belonging to and serving something larger than the self.

**Assessment:** "Do you feel your life has purpose and meaning?"
**Interventions:** Ikigai, legacy project, volunteer work, values clarification, best possible self.

### A — Accomplishment

Pursuing achievement and mastery for their own sake.

**Assessment:** "Are you making progress toward goals that matter to you?"
**Interventions:** SMART goals aligned to values, building mastery (DBT), celebrating wins, strengths-based goal setting.

### PERMA profiler

Rate each domain 1-10. Identify strongest (leverage) and weakest (growth opportunity). Build interventions targeting the lowest-scoring domain first for maximum impact.

## Common mistakes

- Using positive psychology to bypass genuine suffering ("toxic positivity")
- Forcing gratitude when the client is grieving or in crisis
- Confusing "positive psychology" with "think positive" — it's empirical, not motivational
- Ignoring that positive interventions have contraindications (e.g., benefit finding too early after trauma)
- Applying strengths work without validated assessment (VIA survey)
- Neglecting that cultural context shapes what "flourishing" means
- Using PERMA as prescription rather than framework — all five domains don't need to be maximized
