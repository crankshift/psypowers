# dbt-techniques

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) was developed by Marsha Linehan in the late 1980s originally for chronically suicidal individuals with borderline personality disorder (BPD). It has since been adapted for emotion dysregulation broadly, including substance use, eating disorders, treatment-resistant depression, and PTSD. DBT rests on a dialectical philosophy: the core dialectic is the simultaneous need for acceptance (you are doing the best you can) and change (you need to do better). Treatment integrates cognitive-behavioral interventions with Eastern mindfulness practices and dialectical thinking. Standard DBT includes four components: individual therapy, skills group, phone coaching, and a therapist consultation team. The skills themselves are organized into four modules.

## Module 1 -- Mindfulness (Core Skill)

Mindfulness is the foundation of all other DBT skills. It is practiced at the start of every skills group session and is considered the "core" module that supports the other three. The goal is to develop the capacity to be aware and present without judgment.

### Wise Mind

Wise mind is the synthesis of emotion mind and reasonable mind.

- **Emotion mind:** Decisions and perceptions driven by feelings. Hot, reactive, urgent. Thoughts are amplified by mood. Facts may be distorted or ignored. "I feel it, so it must be true."
- **Reasonable mind:** Decisions driven by logic, facts, and rationality. Cool, task-focused. Disconnected from feelings. "The data says X, so I should do X."
- **Wise mind:** The integration of both — knowing something to be true both intellectually and intuitively. It is often experienced as a calm, centered sense of "knowing." Accessed through practice, not force.

**Accessing wise mind:**
- Ask: "Is this my emotion mind or my reasonable mind? What would wise mind say?"
- Stone on the lake visualization: imagine your mind is a lake. Drop a stone (the question) and let it drift down to the calm center. What do you find there?
- Breathing: inhale "wise," exhale "mind" — settle into a centered state
- Wise mind is not a feeling — it is a place from which you can observe both thoughts and feelings

### "What" Skills

These describe what you do when practicing mindfulness:

**Observe:** Notice your experience without reacting to it. See, hear, smell, taste, and touch. Notice thoughts as thoughts, feelings as feelings, urges as urges — not as commands to act. Like watching clouds pass across the sky. Do not push away, cling to, or judge what you observe.

**Describe:** Put words on your experience. "I notice tightness in my chest." "I'm having the thought that I'm going to fail." "I feel sadness." Stick to the facts of the experience. Describing is not interpreting — "my chest is tight" (describe) vs. "I'm stressed because my boss hates me" (interpret).

**Participate:** Throw yourself fully into the current activity. Become one with the experience. Dance without self-consciousness, sing without worrying about your voice, engage in conversation fully. The opposite of observing from the outside — you are in it. This is "flow."

### "How" Skills

These describe how you practice mindfulness:

**Non-judgmentally:** Drop evaluations of "good" and "bad." Replace judgments with descriptions. Not "this is a terrible day" but "this is a day when several things went differently than planned." Notice when you are judging — and don't judge the judging. Simply note "I'm judging" and return to describing.

**One-mindfully:** Do one thing at a time. When eating, eat. When walking, walk. When worrying, worry — then stop and do the next thing. The opposite of multitasking. When your mind wanders, gently bring it back to the one activity.

**Effectively:** Focus on what works rather than what is "right" or "fair." Play by the rules of the current situation even if you disagree with them. Let go of vengeance, useless anger, and righteousness when they interfere with your goals. Ask: "What is my goal in this situation? What will achieve it?"

### Mindfulness Exercises

- **Brief mindfulness (1-3 min):** One minute of observing breath, noticing five things you can see, or three mindful bites of food. Ideal for starting every session or as a between-session practice.
- **Mindful breathing (5-10 min):** Attention on breath at the nostrils or belly. When mind wanders, gently return. Count breaths 1-10 if helpful.
- **Mindful observation (5 min):** Choose one object and observe it with full attention as if seeing it for the first time. Notice color, shape, texture, weight, temperature.

## Module 2 -- Distress Tolerance

Distress tolerance skills are for crisis moments — when the goal is to survive the moment without making things worse. They are not about solving the problem or feeling better. They are about getting through.

### Crisis Survival Skills

**TIPP — rapidly changes body chemistry to reduce extreme emotion (works in seconds to minutes):**

- **Temperature:** Trigger the dive reflex by holding your breath and putting your face in a bowl of cold water (around 50F/10C) for 30 seconds, or hold an ice pack on your face (eyes and cheeks). This rapidly lowers heart rate and calms the nervous system. Caution: avoid with heart conditions; consult physician first.
- **Intense exercise:** 20 minutes of vigorous exercise (running, jumping jacks, fast walking) burns off the fight-or-flight neurochemistry. Match exercise intensity to emotion intensity.
- **Paced breathing:** Slow your breathing to 5-6 breaths per minute. Exhale longer than you inhale (e.g., inhale 4 seconds, exhale 6-8 seconds). Activates the parasympathetic nervous system.
- **Progressive/Paired muscle relaxation:** Systematically tense and release muscle groups while pairing the release with slow exhalation. Tense for 5 seconds, release for 10-15 seconds.

**STOP — prevents impulsive action in the heat of the moment:**

- **Stop:** Literally freeze. Do not move. Do not react.
- **Take a step back:** Remove yourself from the situation mentally or physically. Take a breath. Give yourself a moment.
- **Observe:** What is happening inside you? What is happening around you? What is the other person doing/saying? What are the facts?
- **Proceed mindfully:** Ask wise mind: "What action will be most effective right now? What are my goals? What choice will I not regret?"

### Pros and Cons

A structured analysis of tolerating vs. not tolerating distress. Create a 2x2 grid:
- Pros of tolerating the crisis (acting skillfully)
- Cons of tolerating the crisis (what's hard about it)
- Pros of not tolerating the crisis (giving in to the urge)
- Cons of not tolerating the crisis (what will happen if I act on the urge)

Write this out during non-crisis times and keep it accessible (wallet card, phone note). During a crisis, re-read it — do not try to create it in the moment.

### Radical Acceptance

Acceptance of reality as it is, completely and totally, without fighting it, when reality cannot be changed. Radical acceptance is not approval, agreement, or passivity. It is acknowledging "this is what is."

**Core principles:**
- Pain is inevitable; suffering = pain + non-acceptance
- Refusing to accept reality does not change reality — it only adds suffering
- Acceptance is a choice that must be made over and over — turning the mind toward acceptance repeatedly

**Turning the mind:** The moment between rejecting reality and accepting it. Each time you notice yourself fighting what is, gently turn your mind back toward acceptance. Like turning a boat into the current, again and again.

**Willingness vs. willfulness:**
- *Willingness:* Being open to participating in life, doing what is needed in the present moment, responding to the situation as it is
- *Willfulness:* Refusing to tolerate the moment, insisting that reality be different, trying to control the uncontrollable, giving up

### Self-Soothe with Five Senses

Using each sense to create comfort during distress:

- **Vision:** Look at nature, beautiful art, photos of loved ones, light a candle and watch the flame, watch the stars
- **Hearing:** Listen to calming music, nature sounds, ASMR, a beloved podcast, birds singing, wind chimes
- **Smell:** Light scented candles, use essential oils, smell fresh flowers, bake something, use a favorite lotion
- **Taste:** Drink herbal tea slowly, eat a piece of chocolate mindfully, suck on a mint, savor a favorite food
- **Touch:** Take a warm bath, wrap in a soft blanket, hold a smooth stone, pet an animal, put on comfortable clothes

### IMPROVE the Moment

**I** — Imagery: Imagine a safe, peaceful place in vivid detail. Imagine coping well. Imagine the crisis passing.
**M** — Meaning: Find purpose or meaning in the pain. "What can I learn from this? How might this make me stronger?"
**P** — Prayer: Connect to something larger — formal prayer, meditation, or simply asking for strength.
**R** — Relaxation: Consciously relax muscles, take a warm bath, do gentle stretching.
**O** — One thing in the moment: Focus entirely on what you are doing right now, this exact moment.
**V** — Vacation: Take a brief mental or physical vacation — a 20-minute walk, a hot bath, a chapter of a novel. Not permanent escape, but a short break.
**E** — Encouragement: Talk to yourself as you would talk to a friend. "I can get through this." "This feeling will pass." "I've survived worse."

### Distraction (ACCEPTS)

**A** — Activities: Engage in an activity that demands attention (puzzles, cleaning, calling a friend, exercise).
**C** — Contributing: Do something for someone else (volunteer, send an encouraging text, do a chore for a housemate).
**C** — Comparisons: Compare to a time you coped with something harder, or to others who are less fortunate (use carefully — this is not to minimize your pain but to gain perspective).
**E** — Emotions (opposite): Generate an opposite emotion — watch a comedy when sad, listen to calming music when agitated, look at cute animal pictures when angry.
**P** — Pushing away: Mentally put the crisis on a shelf, in a box, or behind a wall — temporarily. You will deal with it, but not right this second.
**T** — Thoughts: Replace current thoughts with other mental activity — count to 100, recite lyrics, do mental arithmetic, name all the countries you can think of.
**S** — Sensations: Use intense sensory input to interrupt emotional escalation — hold ice cubes, snap a rubber band on your wrist, eat something very sour or spicy, take a cold shower.

## Module 3 -- Emotion Regulation

These skills are for when you are not in crisis but want to reduce emotional vulnerability and manage emotions more effectively over time.

### Identifying and Naming Emotions

You cannot regulate what you cannot identify. Use an emotion wheel (concentric circles from basic emotions in the center to nuanced ones at the edges) to build emotional vocabulary.

**Primary vs. secondary emotions:**
- Primary: the first, most direct emotional response to a situation (e.g., sadness at a loss)
- Secondary: the emotion about the emotion (e.g., anger at yourself for feeling sad, shame about feeling anxious)
- DBT works to identify and validate the primary emotion while examining whether secondary emotions are adding unnecessary suffering

**Components of an emotion:** Each emotion has (1) a prompting event, (2) an interpretation of the event, (3) body sensations, (4) an action urge, and (5) an expressive behavior. Mapping all five components makes the emotion less overwhelming and more workable.

### Check the Facts

Systematically evaluate whether your emotional response fits the situation.

**Steps:**
1. What is the emotion I want to change?
2. What is the prompting event? Describe only the facts — what a camera would record.
3. What are my interpretations, assumptions, and beliefs about the event?
4. Am I assuming a threat? What is the actual probability?
5. What is the catastrophe I'm imagining? Can I cope with the realistic outcome?
6. Does the intensity and duration of my emotion fit the actual facts?

If the emotion fits the facts, use problem solving. If it does not fit the facts, use opposite action.

### Opposite Action

When an emotion is not justified by the facts, or when acting on the emotion urge will make things worse, act opposite to the emotion urge — all the way.

| Emotion | Urge | Opposite Action |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| Fear (unjustified) | Avoid, run, freeze | Approach what you fear. Stay in the situation. Do it again. |
| Anger (unjustified) | Attack, lash out | Gently avoid (not out of fear). Be kind. Take a time-out. Empathize. |
| Sadness (unjustified) | Withdraw, isolate, go to bed | Get active. Engage socially. Do things that build mastery. |
| Shame (unjustified) | Hide, avoid, keep secret | Make it public. Talk about it. Hold your head up. Repeat the behavior. |
| Guilt (unjustified) | Apologize, repair, punish self | Do what you feel guilty about again (if it aligns with your values). Do not apologize or overly repair. |

"All the way" means acting opposite not just in behavior but in body posture, facial expression, and vocal tone.

### Problem Solving

When the emotion fits the facts — the situation genuinely needs to change:
1. Describe the problem specifically
2. Check the facts (is it solvable?)
3. Brainstorm solutions (quantity over quality)
4. Evaluate pros and cons of each solution
5. Choose a solution and plan implementation
6. Act on the plan
7. Evaluate the result

### ABC PLEASE — Reducing Emotional Vulnerability

Long-term strategies for building a life less vulnerable to emotional crises:

**A** — Accumulate positive experiences: schedule one pleasant activity daily (short-term); identify values and build a life worth living around them (long-term).
**B** — Build mastery: do one thing each day that gives a sense of competence and self-efficacy. Start achievable and increase gradually.
**C** — Cope ahead: rehearse coping with anticipated stressful situations. Imagine the situation in detail, then imagine yourself using skills effectively.

**PLEASE — physical health:**
- **PL** — treat PhysicaL illness: see doctors, take medications, address chronic conditions
- **E** — Eat balanced: regular meals, adequate nutrition, avoid extremes of restriction or bingeing
- **A** — Avoid mood-altering substances: drugs and alcohol destabilize emotion regulation
- **S** — Sleep hygiene: consistent sleep/wake times, 7-9 hours, dark/cool room, no screens before bed
- **E** — Exercise: 20+ minutes of cardiovascular exercise most days — one of the most potent emotion regulation interventions available

## Module 4 -- Interpersonal Effectiveness

Skills for navigating relationships while maintaining both the relationship and your self-respect.

### DEAR MAN (Getting What You Want)

Use when you have a specific objective in an interpersonal situation — making a request or saying no.

**D** — Describe: State the facts of the situation objectively. "You said you would call me back yesterday, and you didn't."
**E** — Express: Share how you feel or think about the situation using "I" statements. "I felt worried and unimportant."
**A** — Assert: Ask for what you want or say no clearly. "I would like you to follow through when you say you'll call."
**R** — Reinforce: Explain the positive consequences of getting what you're asking for. "If I know I can count on you, I'll feel more secure in our relationship."

**M** — Mindful: Stay focused on your objective. Do not get sidetracked by attacks, digressions, or past grievances. Broken record: calmly repeat your request.
**A** — Appear confident: Maintain eye contact, speak clearly, use a confident tone and posture — even if you don't feel confident. No mumbling, no excessive qualifying.
**N** — Negotiate: Be willing to give to get. Offer alternatives. Ask: "What do you think would work?" Reduce your request if needed, but don't abandon it entirely. Turn the tables: "What do you think we should do?"

### GIVE (Maintaining the Relationship)

Use when the priority is preserving or strengthening the relationship, even at some cost to your objective.

**G** — Gentle: No attacks, threats, or judgments. No "you always" or "you never." Drop the sharp edges.
**I** — Interested: Listen. Ask questions. Don't interrupt. Show genuine curiosity about the other person's perspective. Lean in.
**V** — Validate: Acknowledge the other person's feelings, thoughts, and experiences as understandable. "I can see why you would feel that way." Validation does not mean agreement.
**E** — Easy manner: Use humor. Smile. Be light. Don't take yourself too seriously. Make the interaction pleasant. Leave the heaviness at the door when you can.

### FAST (Maintaining Self-Respect)

Use when the priority is keeping your self-respect intact, regardless of the outcome.

**F** — Fair: Be fair to both yourself and the other person. Don't be a doormat, and don't be a bully.
**A** — no Apologies (when unnecessary): Don't apologize for existing, having an opinion, making a request, or disagreeing. Apologize when you've genuinely done something wrong — not as a reflex.
**S** — Stick to values: Don't abandon your values to get what you want or to please someone. If something violates your principles, say no — even if it's hard.
**T** — Truthful: Don't lie, exaggerate, or act helpless. Be honest about what you want, what you feel, and what you think. Dishonesty erodes self-respect over time.

### Factors That Reduce Interpersonal Effectiveness

- **Lack of skill:** You simply don't know what to say or do — skills training addresses this
- **Worry thoughts:** "They'll be angry," "I don't deserve to ask," "What if they say no?" — use cognitive restructuring
- **Emotions:** Intense emotions (anxiety, anger, shame) override skillful behavior — regulate the emotion first
- **Indecision:** Not knowing what you actually want — clarify priorities (objective? relationship? self-respect?) before the interaction
- **Environment:** The other person is genuinely powerful, hostile, or unreasonable — sometimes the environment is the problem, and the most effective thing is to change the environment, not your approach

### Prioritizing: Objective, Relationship, or Self-Respect

Before an interpersonal interaction, decide which priority matters most:
- If **objective** is most important → lead with DEAR MAN
- If **relationship** is most important → lead with GIVE
- If **self-respect** is most important → lead with FAST
- Most interactions require all three — but knowing the priority determines which skills to emphasize when they conflict
