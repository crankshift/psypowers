# mindfulness-practices

Mindfulness is the practice of paying attention to the present moment, on purpose, and non-judgmentally (Jon Kabat-Zinn). It is both a standalone intervention tradition (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction / MBSR, Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy / MBCT) and a core component of DBT and ACT. The evidence base supports mindfulness for anxiety, depression, chronic pain, stress, insomnia, emotion dysregulation, and relapse prevention in depression and substance use. Mindfulness is a skill, not a trait — it strengthens with practice and atrophies without it. This skill file provides detailed scripts and instructions for a range of practices suitable for clinical recommendation, organized by primary modality.

## Breath-Based Practices

### Breath Awareness

The foundational mindfulness practice. No manipulation of the breath — simply observing natural breathing.

**Instructions:** Sit comfortably with feet flat on the floor. Close your eyes or lower your gaze. Bring your attention to your breathing. Choose an anchor point — the sensation of air at the nostrils, the rise and fall of the chest, or the expansion and contraction of the belly. Simply notice the breath as it comes and goes. When your mind wanders — and it will — gently notice where it went and return your attention to the breath. No judgment about the wandering. Each return is the practice.

**Duration variants:**
- **2 minutes:** Suitable for complete beginners or acute moments. "Take five slow breaths, noticing each one fully."
- **5 minutes:** Standard introductory practice. Set a timer. Aim for this daily for the first 1-2 weeks.
- **10 minutes:** Building practice. Most clients can sustain this within 2-3 weeks.
- **20 minutes:** Full practice. The MBSR standard for daily home practice. Significant benefits emerge at this duration with consistency.

**Anchor selection:** Nostrils work best for focused attention (the sensation is subtle, demanding concentration). Belly works best for clients with anxiety (belly-focused attention tends to be calming and promotes diaphragmatic breathing). Chest is a middle ground. Let the client experiment and choose.

**Common difficulties:**
- "I can't stop thinking" — normal; the practice is not stopping thoughts but noticing them and returning. Thoughts are not failure.
- "I fall asleep" — open your eyes slightly, sit upright rather than lying down, practice at a different time of day
- "I feel more anxious" — switch from eyes closed to eyes open with a soft gaze on the floor; shorten the practice; try a different anchor (belly breathing tends to be less activating)

### Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)

A structured breathing pattern that creates a sense of control and calm. Used by military, first responders, and athletes for acute stress management.

**Instructions:**
1. Inhale through the nose for 4 seconds
2. Hold the breath for 4 seconds
3. Exhale through the mouth for 4 seconds
4. Hold the breath (lungs empty) for 4 seconds
5. Repeat for 4-8 cycles

**Best for:** Acute anxiety, pre-performance nerves, moments when you need rapid self-regulation. The holds introduce a sense of control and interrupt rapid anxious breathing.

**Adaptations:** If 4 seconds is too long, start with 3-3-3-3 and build up. If the hold after exhale is uncomfortable, reduce it to 2 seconds. Clients with respiratory conditions should skip the breath holds.

### 4-7-8 Breathing (Andrew Weil)

A pattern specifically designed to activate the parasympathetic nervous system. The extended exhale and long hold shift the autonomic balance toward calm.

**Instructions:**
1. Exhale completely through the mouth
2. Close the mouth and inhale quietly through the nose for 4 seconds
3. Hold the breath for 7 seconds
4. Exhale completely through the mouth for 8 seconds (making a whoosh sound)
5. This is one cycle. Repeat for 4 cycles.

**Best for:** Sleep onset insomnia, deep relaxation, winding down. Many clients find this is the single most effective breathing technique for falling asleep. Practice twice daily and use at bedtime.

**Caution:** The long hold can feel uncomfortable or trigger anxiety in some clients. Build up gradually — start with 4-4-6 if needed and work toward the full pattern over 2-3 weeks.

### Counting Breaths

A concentration-focused practice that gives the mind a "job," reducing mind-wandering.

**Instructions:** Breathe naturally. On each exhale, count: 1... 2... 3... up to 10. Then restart at 1. If you lose count or catch yourself at 15, simply return to 1 without judgment. The losing count and returning is the practice.

**Duration:** 5-15 minutes. Useful as a step between breath awareness (which some find too unstructured) and more advanced practices.

### Coherent Breathing (5-5 Pattern)

Breathing at approximately 6 breaths per minute (5 seconds in, 5 seconds out) has been shown to optimize heart rate variability (HRV), which is associated with emotional resilience and autonomic flexibility.

**Instructions:** Inhale for 5 seconds. Exhale for 5 seconds. No pauses between breaths — continuous, smooth flow. Breathe through the nose if possible. Maintain for 10-20 minutes.

**Best for:** Daily practice for chronic stress, building long-term autonomic resilience, anxiety disorders, PTSD. Can be done with a pacing app or metronome for precision.

**Key point:** The specific rate matters. Many people breathe at 12-20 breaths per minute. Slowing to 6 breaths per minute (the "resonance frequency") produces the strongest HRV increase. Some individuals have a slightly different resonance frequency (4.5-7 breaths/min) — HRV biofeedback can identify the individual optimum.

## Body-Based Practices

### Body Scan

A systematic attention practice moving through the body, noticing sensations without trying to change them.

**Full body scan (30-45 min):**
Starting at the toes of the left foot, bring attention to each body part in sequence. Notice whatever sensations are present — tingling, warmth, pressure, numbness, pain, nothing at all. Spend 1-3 minutes on each region. Sequence: left toes → left foot → left ankle → left lower leg → left knee → left upper leg → right toes → right foot → right ankle → right lower leg → right knee → right upper leg → pelvis/hips → lower back → abdomen → upper back → chest → left fingers → left hand → left wrist → left forearm → left upper arm → right fingers → right hand → right wrist → right forearm → right upper arm → shoulders → neck → jaw → face → forehead → scalp → entire body as a whole.

**Abbreviated body scan (10-15 min):**
Same principle with larger regions: feet → legs → pelvis/hips → torso (front and back) → hands and arms → shoulders and neck → head and face → whole body.

**Ultra-brief body scan (3-5 min):**
Quick sweep: feet → legs → torso → arms → head → whole body. Spend about 30 seconds per region.

**Clinical considerations:**
- *Trauma survivors:* Body scans may be activating — the focus on internal sensations can trigger trauma responses. Start with the ultra-brief version, keep eyes open, start from the feet (furthest from the emotional centers), and give the client permission to skip any body region that feels unsafe. Offer an external anchor ("If at any point this becomes too intense, open your eyes and look at something in the room").
- *Chronic pain:* Body scans can be powerful for changing the relationship to pain, but they require careful framing. The goal is not to reduce pain but to notice it with curiosity rather than fear. Clients often discover that "pain" is actually multiple sensations (heat, pressure, pulsing) and that it changes moment to moment.
- *Dissociation:* For clients who tend to dissociate, body scans may increase disconnection. Use shorter versions, keep eyes open, include movement (wiggle fingers/toes at each station), and maintain verbal contact throughout.

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR)

A technique developed by Edmund Jacobson that systematically tenses and releases muscle groups, training the body to recognize and release tension.

**Full PMR (16 muscle groups, 20-30 min):**
For each group: tense the muscles firmly (but not painfully) for 5-7 seconds, then release suddenly and notice the relaxation for 15-20 seconds. Focus on the contrast between tension and relaxation.

Sequence: (1) dominant hand/forearm (make a fist), (2) dominant upper arm (bicep curl), (3) non-dominant hand/forearm, (4) non-dominant upper arm, (5) forehead (raise eyebrows), (6) eyes and nose (squint tightly), (7) jaw and lower face (clench jaw, press tongue to roof of mouth), (8) neck (press head back against chair or pull chin to chest), (9) chest and upper back (take deep breath, hold, pull shoulder blades together), (10) abdomen (tighten stomach muscles), (11) dominant upper leg (tighten thigh), (12) dominant lower leg (point toes up toward shin), (13) dominant foot (curl toes down), (14) non-dominant upper leg, (15) non-dominant lower leg, (16) non-dominant foot.

**Abbreviated PMR (7 muscle groups, 10-15 min):**
Combine: (1) both hands and arms, (2) face, (3) neck, (4) chest/back/abdomen, (5) both upper legs, (6) both lower legs, (7) both feet.

**Brief PMR (4 muscle groups, 5-7 min):**
Combine: (1) both arms and hands, (2) face and neck, (3) torso, (4) both legs and feet.

**Progression:** Start with full 16-group PMR for 1-2 weeks. Move to 7-group for 1-2 weeks. Then to 4-group. Eventually, clients learn to release tension without the tense phase ("release-only relaxation" or "cue-controlled relaxation" — pairing a cue word like "relax" or "calm" with the release).

## Awareness Practices

### 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

A rapid grounding technique that anchors attention in the external world through the senses. Highly effective for dissociation, panic attacks, flashbacks, and derealization/depersonalization.

**Instructions:**
Name aloud or silently:
- **5** things you can **see** (a crack in the ceiling, the color of the wall, a coffee stain on the desk, the pattern on your shirt, a tree outside the window)
- **4** things you can **hear** (traffic, the hum of a heater, a voice in the next room, your own breathing)
- **3** things you can **touch** or physically feel (feet on the floor, the texture of your jeans, the weight of your hands in your lap)
- **2** things you can **smell** (coffee, soap on your hands — if you can't smell anything, name 2 things you like the smell of)
- **1** thing you can **taste** (the aftertaste of lunch, toothpaste — or name 1 thing you enjoy tasting)

**Clinical use:** Teach this early in therapy for any client prone to dissociation, panic, or flashbacks. It can be used anywhere, requires no equipment, and works within 1-2 minutes. Encourage specific, detailed answers rather than generic ones — specificity deepens the grounding effect.

### Noting/Labeling Practice

Silently labeling the type of mental activity occurring. This meta-cognitive practice builds the capacity to observe the mind's activity without being swept into it.

**Instructions:** Sit quietly and notice whatever arises. When you notice a thought, silently label it "thinking." When you notice a sound, label it "hearing." When you notice a body sensation, label it "feeling." When you notice planning, label it "planning." When you notice remembering, label it "remembering." When you notice worrying, label it "worrying." Keep labels simple and neutral — one or two words. Then return to open awareness.

**Advanced variant:** Use more precise labels — "judging," "rehearsing," "fantasizing," "comparing," "predicting," "analyzing." The precision builds granular meta-cognitive awareness.

**Duration:** 10-20 minutes. Can be combined with breath awareness (return to breath between notes).

### Open Monitoring

A practice of sitting with awareness open to whatever arises, without selecting any particular object of attention.

**Instructions:** Sit comfortably. Instead of focusing on the breath or any single object, let your attention be open and receptive — like a wide-angle lens rather than a zoom lens. Notice whatever comes into awareness — sounds, sensations, thoughts, emotions — and let it pass. Don't follow anything. Don't hold on to anything. Just notice the flow.

**Prerequisites:** Open monitoring is significantly more difficult than focused attention practices. Clients should have at least 4-6 weeks of breath awareness practice before attempting open monitoring. Without a stable attention foundation, open monitoring tends to become rumination.

### Choiceless Awareness

An advanced form of open monitoring where attention is free to move wherever it is drawn — the practitioner simply notices what captures attention and observes it until attention moves elsewhere.

**Instructions:** Sit with eyes closed or gaze lowered. Let attention rest wherever it naturally goes. If drawn to a sound, rest with the sound. If drawn to a body sensation, rest with the sensation. If drawn to a thought, notice the thought. Don't direct attention — let it be directed by whatever is most prominent in your experience. The practice is complete receptivity.

**Best for:** Experienced practitioners (3+ months of regular practice). Deepens insight into the nature of attention and the transient quality of all experience.

## Compassion Practices

### Loving-Kindness Meditation (Metta)

A practice of generating feelings of warmth, friendliness, and care — first toward yourself, then expanding outward.

**Traditional phrases:**
- "May I be safe."
- "May I be happy."
- "May I be healthy."
- "May I live with ease."

**Sequence of recipients (5 stages):**
1. **Self:** Direct the phrases toward yourself. This is often the hardest stage. If resistance arises, notice it with compassion and continue. (3-5 min)
2. **Loved one:** Someone you care about deeply and naturally feel warmth toward. Picture them clearly. "May you be safe. May you be happy. May you be healthy. May you live with ease." (3-5 min)
3. **Neutral person:** Someone you neither like nor dislike — a neighbor, a cashier, someone you pass on the street. This stage develops equanimity. (3-5 min)
4. **Difficult person:** Someone you have conflict with or negative feelings toward. Start with someone mildly difficult, not your worst enemy. This stage is the stretch. (3-5 min)
5. **All beings:** Expand the phrases to encompass everyone — "May all beings be safe, happy, healthy, and live with ease." Radiate compassion outward in all directions. (3-5 min)

**Duration:** Full practice is 15-25 minutes. Shortened versions focus on 1-2 stages. Beginners can start with self + loved one only.

**Adaptations:**
- Clients who struggle with self-compassion: start with the loved one stage and gradually introduce self-directed phrases over several weeks
- Customize phrases to what resonates: "May I be peaceful," "May I be free from suffering," "May I accept myself as I am"
- Some clients prefer to visualize warmth or light radiating from the heart rather than using phrases

### Self-Compassion Break (Kristin Neff)

A brief (2-3 minute) structured practice for moments of suffering, based on the three components of self-compassion.

**Step 1 — Mindfulness (acknowledge suffering):** Place your hand on your heart or another soothing touch point. Say to yourself: "This is a moment of suffering." Or: "This hurts." Or: "This is hard right now." This acknowledges the pain without exaggerating or minimizing it.

**Step 2 — Common humanity:** "Suffering is part of being human." Or: "I'm not alone in this — everyone struggles." Or: "Other people feel this way too." This counteracts the isolation that suffering creates — the sense that you are the only one, that something is wrong with you specifically.

**Step 3 — Self-kindness:** "May I be kind to myself." Or: "May I give myself the compassion I need." Or: "What would I say to a dear friend in this situation?" This actively directs care toward yourself rather than criticism.

**Clinical use:** Teach this to any client with harsh self-criticism, perfectionistic standards, or shame-based difficulties. It can be used in the moment — during a stressful meeting, after a perceived failure, during a difficult emotion.

### Compassionate Body Scan

A body scan infused with the intention of care and warmth. As attention moves through each body region, the practitioner sends kindness to that area — thanking the body for what it does, offering warmth to areas of tension or pain, and releasing judgment about the body.

**Especially useful for:** Clients with chronic pain, body image concerns, trauma stored in the body, or those who find standard body scans too clinical or activating.

## Movement-Based Practices

### Mindful Walking

**Instructions:** Walk at a slow, deliberate pace — about one-quarter of your normal speed. Bring full attention to each component of walking: lifting the foot, moving the foot forward, placing the foot down, shifting weight. Notice the sensations in the soles of the feet, the movement of the legs, the balancing of the body. When the mind wanders, return to the physical sensations of walking.

**Variants:**
- *Indoor:* Walk a 10-20 foot path back and forth. Pace: very slow. Duration: 10-20 minutes.
- *Outdoor:* Walk a familiar route at a moderately slow pace. Include awareness of environment — sounds, air, temperature, visual field. Duration: 15-30 minutes.
- *Integration with breath:* Match steps to breath — inhale for 3-4 steps, exhale for 3-4 steps.

**Best for:** Clients who find sitting practice physically uncomfortable, those who are very physically active and need a bridge to stillness, restless or agitated clients, walking meditation as part of a longer sitting-walking-sitting cycle.

### Mindful Eating

**Raisin exercise (classic MBSR):** Take a single raisin. Hold it. Examine it visually as if you've never seen a raisin before — notice the color, texture, ridges, how light reflects off it. Feel its texture between your fingers. Its weight. Bring it slowly to your nose and smell it. Notice any anticipation in the mouth (salivation). Place it on your tongue without chewing. Notice the sensation of it sitting there. Slowly begin to chew — one chew at a time. Notice the explosion of flavor, the changing texture. Swallow when ready, and notice the sensation of swallowing. Follow the raisin down as far as you can.

This exercise typically takes 5-10 minutes for a single raisin. It dramatically illustrates the difference between automatic, mindless eating and present-moment eating. Applicable to any food or an entire meal.

### Mindful Stretching

Gentle, slow movements performed with complete attention to body sensations. Not yoga for exercise — yoga for awareness. Each stretch is held for 30-60 seconds with full attention to the sensations of stretching, the breath, and the moment-to-moment changes in the body. Useful as a transition practice between activity and sitting meditation, or as a standalone 5-10 minute practice.

## RAIN Technique (Tara Brach)

A four-step mindfulness practice for working with difficult emotions. Structured enough to provide a clear path, flexible enough to apply to any emotional experience.

**R — Recognize:** What is happening inside me right now? Name the emotion, the body sensation, the thought pattern. "I'm feeling anxious. My chest is tight. My mind is racing about tomorrow." Simply seeing clearly what is present.

**A — Allow:** Let the experience be here without trying to fix, change, avoid, or judge it. This is not passive resignation — it is active permission. Say internally: "This belongs here too." Or simply: "Yes." Or: "Let me feel this." Allow does not mean like or want — it means stop fighting.

**I — Investigate:** With genuine curiosity and kindness, explore the experience more deeply. Where do I feel this most strongly in my body? What does this feeling believe? What does this part of me most need? What is the youngest, most vulnerable place this is coming from? Investigation is warm and gentle, not analytical or interrogating.

**N — Nurture:** What does this suffering part of me need to hear? Offer care, the way you would to a frightened child or a dear friend. Place a hand on your heart. Say: "I'm here with you." Or: "It's okay — you're safe now." Or: "I care about this pain." Let yourself receive the nurturing, even if it feels unfamiliar.

**After RAIN:** Sit quietly for a moment and notice what has shifted. Often there is a sense of spaciousness, softening, or relief — not because the difficulty is gone, but because you are no longer alone with it.

**Duration:** 10-20 minutes for the full process. Can be abbreviated (5 min for a lighter touch) or extended (30+ min for deep emotional work).

**Clinical applications:**
- Anxiety: RAIN helps clients turn toward anxiety rather than away from it, reducing the secondary fear-of-fear cycle
- Shame: The Nurture step is particularly powerful for shame, which thrives in isolation and withers in the presence of compassion
- Trauma: Use with care — Investigation can be activating. Titrate depth. Keep the Nurture step robust.
- Depression: Can help access underlying emotions (grief, anger, helplessness) that depression may be covering over

**Contraindications for all mindfulness practices:**
- Active psychosis or acute mania: mindfulness can increase disorganization
- Severe dissociation: may deepen disconnection; use grounding practices instead
- Acute trauma: formal sitting practice may be overwhelming; use brief, externally-focused practices (5-4-3-2-1, mindful walking)
- Suicidal crisis: mindfulness is not a crisis intervention; use safety planning, distress tolerance skills, and clinical escalation
