# somatic-practices

Somatic approaches to therapy recognize that trauma, stress, and emotional experience are not purely cognitive phenomena — they are fundamentally embodied. The body holds patterns of tension, bracing, constriction, and collapse that persist long after the original stressor has passed. These patterns operate below conscious awareness and are not fully accessible through talk therapy alone. Somatic practices draw from multiple lineages: Somatic Experiencing (Peter Levine), Sensorimotor Psychotherapy (Pat Ogden), polyvagal theory (Stephen Porges), and traditional body-based practices adapted for clinical use. The core principle across all somatic approaches: the body has an innate capacity for self-regulation and healing — the therapeutic task is to support and restore that capacity, not to override it.

## Window of Tolerance (Dan Siegel)

The window of tolerance is the zone of autonomic arousal within which a person can function effectively — able to think clearly, feel emotions without being overwhelmed, and engage with others. When arousal exceeds the window (hyperarousal) or drops below it (hypoarousal), the capacity for integrated functioning breaks down. Trauma narrows the window; therapy widens it.

### Three Zones of Arousal

**Optimal arousal zone (within the window):**
- Alert and present
- Able to think and feel simultaneously
- Can process information and make decisions
- Emotionally responsive but not overwhelmed
- Socially engaged
- Flexible in responses
- Can tolerate discomfort without dysregulation

**Hyperarousal (above the window) — sympathetic dominance / fight-flight:**

| Domain | Signs |
|--------|-------|
| Physical | Rapid heartbeat, shallow/fast breathing, muscle tension, trembling, restlessness, sweating, startle response, jaw clenching |
| Emotional | Anxiety, panic, anger, rage, irritability, hypervigilance, feeling unsafe, overwhelm |
| Cognitive | Racing thoughts, difficulty concentrating, catastrophizing, hyperscanning for threat, intrusive memories, flashbacks |
| Behavioral | Agitation, pacing, aggression, yelling, fleeing, inability to sit still, compulsive activity |

**Hypoarousal (below the window) — dorsal vagal dominance / freeze-collapse:**

| Domain | Signs |
|--------|-------|
| Physical | Low energy, fatigue, heaviness, slowed heart rate, shallow breathing, numbness, reduced pain sensitivity, nausea |
| Emotional | Flat affect, emptiness, emotional numbness, disconnection, hopelessness, shame, helplessness |
| Cognitive | Foggy thinking, difficulty speaking, blank mind, confusion, difficulty making decisions, dissociation, derealization, depersonalization |
| Behavioral | Withdrawal, isolation, immobility, collapse posture (slumped, curled), excessive sleeping, zoning out, staring |

### Strategies to Return to the Window

**From hyperarousal (need to down-regulate):**
- Slow, extended-exhale breathing (inhale 4s, exhale 8s)
- Grounding: feet on floor, hands on solid surfaces, 5-4-3-2-1 sensory grounding
- Bilateral stimulation: butterfly hug, alternating knee taps
- Cold water on face or wrists (activates dive reflex)
- Slow, rhythmic movement: rocking, swaying, slow walking
- Progressive muscle relaxation (particularly jaw, shoulders, hands)
- Orientation: slowly turn your head and look around the room, naming what you see
- Humming or singing (vagal toning)

**From hypoarousal (need to up-regulate):**
- Movement: stand up, walk, stretch, stomp feet, push against a wall
- Strong sensory input: cold water, strong scent (peppermint oil), sour taste (lemon)
- Engage muscles: squeeze and release fists, press palms together firmly
- Eye contact and social engagement (co-regulation with a safe person)
- Orienting: actively look around the room, noticing colors and objects
- Firm touch: press feet into the floor, squeeze your own arms, self-massage
- Voice: speak aloud (narrate what you see), hum, count
- Temperature change: cold washcloth on neck, hold ice

## Somatic Experiencing (SE) Concepts

Somatic Experiencing, developed by Peter Levine, is based on the observation that wild animals routinely experience life-threatening events but rarely develop traumatic stress — because they complete the biological stress response cycle (fight/flight/freeze → discharge → return to baseline). Humans, due to our neocortex, often interrupt this cycle — we override the body's impulse to shake, flee, or fight, and the incomplete response becomes "stuck" in the nervous system as traumatic activation.

### Pendulation

The natural rhythm of the nervous system oscillates between activation (expansion/charge) and settling (contraction/discharge). In trauma, this oscillation gets stuck — a person may be chronically activated or chronically collapsed.

**Practice:**
1. Start with a resource — a body sensation that feels calm, strong, or neutral. This is the "home base."
2. Briefly contact an area of activation — a body sensation connected to stress or distress.
3. Return to the resource before activation becomes overwhelming.
4. Oscillate: resource → activation → resource → activation. Each cycle, the nervous system learns that it can move into distress and return to safety.

**Key principle:** Pendulation builds the nervous system's confidence in its own capacity to regulate. The practitioner titrates the exposure so the client always returns to regulation before being overwhelmed.

### Titration

Processing traumatic material in small, manageable doses — touching the edge of activation and then stepping back, rather than flooding the system with the full traumatic memory.

**Principles:**
- Work with one element at a time: a single sensation, a fragment of a memory, one moment in the sequence
- Move slowly — slower than the client wants to go. Speed is the enemy of trauma processing.
- Watch for signs of overwhelm (hyperarousal) or shutdown (hypoarousal) and slow down or pause before they escalate
- "Less is more" — a session where very little appears to happen externally may produce significant internal reorganization
- The goal is not catharsis but gradual integration

### Resourcing

Identifying and strengthening body-based resources — internal experiences of safety, strength, calm, or competence that can serve as anchors during activation.

**Types of resources:**
- *Somatic resources:* A place in the body that feels calm, strong, warm, or grounded (e.g., "my feet feel solid on the floor," "my belly feels warm and settled")
- *Relational resources:* The felt sense of a safe person — what happens in the body when you think of someone who makes you feel safe?
- *Nature resources:* A place in nature that evokes peace — what do you feel in your body when you imagine being there?
- *Mastery resources:* The body memory of a time you handled something well — what does competence feel like in your body?

**Building the resource:** Have the client describe the resource in sensory detail, notice where it lives in the body, and amplify it by bringing full attention to it for 30-60 seconds. This strengthens the neural pathway to that experience and makes it accessible during activation.

### Completing Defensive Responses

Trauma often involves thwarted fight or flight — the body mobilized to act but was prevented (pinned, frozen, too young, too small). The incomplete motor sequence remains stored in the body.

**Practice:**
- Identify the defensive impulse: "What did your body want to do?" (run, push away, hit, curl up, scream)
- Slowly allow the movement to emerge at low intensity — a slow pushing motion with the hands, a gentle turning away, a small stepping motion
- Support the completion: "Let your arms do what they wanted to do. Slowly. Feel the strength in your hands."
- Observe discharge signs as the response completes

### Discharge Signs

When the nervous system releases stored traumatic energy, it produces observable physical signs. These are healthy and should be normalized, not suppressed:

- **Trembling or shaking** — the body's natural discharge mechanism (same as animals shaking after a threat)
- **Heat or flushing** — vasodilation as the sympathetic nervous system releases
- **Tears** — not necessarily emotional crying; sometimes purely physiological release
- **Deep, spontaneous breaths** — the diaphragm releasing its holding pattern
- **Yawning** — vagal activation, shift toward parasympathetic tone
- **Gurgling stomach/bowel sounds** — digestive system reactivating (fight/flight shuts down digestion)
- **Goosebumps or tingling** — nervous system reorganization
- **Spontaneous movement** — twitching, repositioning, stretching

**Therapeutic response:** When discharge occurs, the practitioner supports it: "Let that happen. That's your body letting go. You're doing exactly what you need to do." Do not stop or interpret the discharge — simply witness it with calm presence.

## Breath Work for Regulation

### Diaphragmatic Breathing

The foundation of somatic breath work. Most people, especially those with chronic stress or trauma, breathe shallowly from the chest (thoracic breathing). Diaphragmatic breathing engages the primary breathing muscle and activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

**Instructions:**
1. Place one hand on your chest, one hand on your belly
2. Breathe in through the nose — the belly hand should rise; the chest hand should stay relatively still
3. Breathe out through the mouth — the belly hand falls
4. The breath should feel effortless, not forced. If the chest is moving more than the belly, try lying down (gravity naturally promotes diaphragmatic breathing in the supine position)

**Practice:** 5-10 minutes, 2-3 times daily. Builds new breathing patterns within 2-4 weeks of consistent practice.

### Extended Exhale

The exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system (via the vagus nerve). By deliberately lengthening the exhale relative to the inhale, you shift the autonomic balance toward calm.

**Instructions:** Inhale normally (e.g., 4 seconds). Exhale for roughly twice as long (e.g., 7-8 seconds). No need to be precise — the principle is simply that the out-breath is noticeably longer than the in-breath. Do not force — the exhale should feel like a natural, slow release, not a strained pushing.

**Best for:** Acute anxiety, pre-sleep, any moment of elevated arousal. Can be done anywhere, invisibly.

### Breath Holds (CO2 Tolerance Training)

Gentle breath holds after the exhale build carbon dioxide tolerance, which reduces the body's sensitivity to the internal cues of anxiety (the "air hunger" feeling that triggers panic).

**Instructions:**
1. Breathe naturally for 1-2 minutes
2. After a normal exhale, gently hold your breath (nose and mouth closed)
3. Hold for 3-5 seconds initially (this should feel easy, not distressing)
4. Resume normal breathing for 30-60 seconds
5. Repeat 3-5 times
6. Gradually increase the hold by 1-2 seconds per week, up to 15-20 seconds

**Caution:** This is not a breath-holding endurance exercise. The hold should be comfortable, producing only mild air hunger. If the client gasps on the inhale, the hold was too long. Not appropriate during panic attacks — use extended exhale instead. Contraindicated in pregnancy and cardiovascular conditions.

## Vagal Toning (Polyvagal-Informed Practices)

Stephen Porges' polyvagal theory identifies three hierarchical circuits of the autonomic nervous system: (1) the ventral vagal complex (social engagement, safety, connection), (2) the sympathetic nervous system (fight/flight, mobilization), and (3) the dorsal vagal complex (freeze, collapse, shutdown). Vagal toning practices strengthen ventral vagal tone — the capacity to feel safe, connected, and regulated.

### Cold Water Exposure

Cold water activates the mammalian dive reflex — an automatic response that slows heart rate and redirects blood to vital organs. This provides rapid parasympathetic activation.

**Methods:**
- Splash cold water on your face, particularly the forehead, eyes, and cheeks (where the trigeminal nerve is most accessible)
- Hold an ice cube in each hand and squeeze for 30-60 seconds
- Place a cold pack or cold wet cloth on the back of the neck
- Cold shower: start with 15-30 seconds of cold water at the end of a warm shower. Build gradually.
- Bowl method: fill a bowl with cold water (50-60F/10-15C), hold breath, and submerge face for 15-30 seconds

**Best for:** Rapid down-regulation from hyperarousal, panic, intense anger, overwhelming emotion. Works within 30-60 seconds.

### Humming, Chanting, and Singing

The vagus nerve runs through the pharynx and larynx. Vibrations from vocalization stimulate the vagus and promote ventral vagal activation.

**Humming:** Hum on the exhale at a comfortable pitch. Feel the vibration in the chest, throat, and face. Hum for 5-10 minutes. The pitch and tune do not matter — the vibration does.

**Chanting:** Sustained "Om" or any vowel sound (aaah, oooh, eee) on the exhale. The longer the sound, the longer the exhale, which adds parasympathetic activation on top of the vibratory effect.

**Singing:** Singing requires sustained exhalation and engages the social engagement system (prosody, facial muscles, laryngeal muscles). Singing in a group adds the co-regulation benefit.

### Gargling

Vigorously gargling water activates the pharyngeal muscles innervated by the vagus nerve. Gargle firmly enough that your eyes water slightly — this indicates strong vagal activation. Practice for 30-60 seconds, 2-3 times daily.

### Voo Breath (Peter Levine)

A deep, sustained "Voooooo" sound on the exhale that creates powerful vibration in the chest and abdomen, stimulating the vagus nerve along its path through the torso.

**Instructions:** Take a full, deep breath. On the exhale, make a deep "Voooooo" sound (like "voodoo" without the "doo"). Feel the vibration in your chest, belly, and pelvis. The sound should be low-pitched and resonant — not high or strained. Continue for the full length of the exhale. Repeat for 5-10 breaths.

**Best for:** Grounding after activation, settling the nervous system, connecting to body sensation in a safe way.

### Social Engagement

The ventral vagal system is fundamentally a social system. Co-regulation — the nervous system's ability to settle in the presence of a safe, regulated other — is the most powerful regulator available.

**Elements of social engagement:**
- Eye contact (warm, not staring)
- Prosodic voice (melodic, varied in pitch — as opposed to flat or monotone)
- Relaxed facial muscles (especially around the eyes)
- Physical proximity with a safe person
- Attuned listening and responding

**Clinical implication:** Before teaching any self-regulation technique, assess whether the client has access to safe co-regulation. Humans are designed to regulate in relationship first, then independently. For clients with relational trauma, the therapeutic relationship itself may be the primary somatic resource.

## Bilateral Stimulation

Alternating stimulation of the left and right sides of the body activates both hemispheres and promotes integration. While EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) is the most researched form, simpler bilateral techniques can be used for self-regulation without full EMDR training.

### Butterfly Hug

**Instructions:**
1. Cross your arms over your chest, fingertips resting on the area just below the collarbones (or on the shoulders)
2. Alternately tap your right hand, then left hand, in a slow, rhythmic pattern (like a butterfly's wings)
3. Tap at a pace that feels soothing — approximately one tap per second on each side
4. While tapping, breathe slowly and think of a safe place, a calming image, or simply notice body sensations
5. Continue for 2-5 minutes

**Best for:** Self-soothing during distress, grounding, transitioning to sleep. Can be done privately and without equipment. Developed by Lucina Artigas for use with hurricane survivors — effective in group and individual settings.

### Alternating Tapping

**Knee taps:** Place hands on knees. Alternately press left hand, right hand, in a steady rhythm. Can be done while sitting anywhere — in a waiting room, at a desk, on a bus.

**Foot taps:** While seated, alternately press left foot, right foot, into the floor. Discreet and effective.

**Shoulder taps (self-administered):** Reach across the body and alternately tap each shoulder.

**Duration:** 2-5 minutes of rhythmic alternating stimulation. Pace: moderate, approximately 1 tap per second per side. Faster is not better — a slow, steady rhythm is more regulating.

### Simple Eye Movements

**Instructions:** Hold a finger or a pen approximately 12-18 inches from your face. Slowly move it horizontally from left to right and back, taking about 1 second per pass. Follow the movement with your eyes while keeping your head still. Continue for 20-30 passes.

**Note:** This is not EMDR — it is a simple bilateral eye movement for regulation. Full EMDR requires specialized training, a structured protocol, and clinical supervision. These simple eye movements can support general regulation but should not be used to process specific traumatic memories outside of a trained EMDR protocol.

## Tension Release Exercises (TRE -- David Berceli)

TRE is a sequence of seven exercises designed to fatigue specific muscle groups and induce neurogenic tremoring — the body's natural mechanism for releasing deep muscular tension and stored stress activation. The tremoring originates from the psoas muscle (the primary muscle of the fight/flight response) and can spread throughout the body.

### The 7-Exercise Sequence

1. **Ankle stretch:** Stand on one foot and raise the other heel. Hold until fatigue tremor begins. Switch sides.
2. **Calf stretch:** Place toes on a raised surface (book/step), lean forward until calves stretch. Hold 2-3 minutes.
3. **Thigh stretch:** Stand with feet wide apart, shift weight to one side into a wide lunge. Hold until thigh trembles. Switch sides.
4. **Wall sit:** Back against wall, slide down until thighs are parallel to floor. Hold until legs shake.
5. **Ground touch:** Stand with feet shoulder-width, bend forward and touch the ground. Hang loosely for 2-3 minutes.
6. **Pelvic lift (bridge):** Lie on back, knees bent, lift pelvis. Hold until tremoring begins in legs.
7. **Tremoring position:** From the bridge, slowly lower pelvis to floor. Bring soles of feet together, knees apart (butterfly position). Slowly bring knees toward each other — this typically triggers spontaneous tremoring in the legs, which may spread to the pelvis, torso, and arms.

### Safety Guidelines

- **Start short:** First sessions should be 5-10 minutes of tremoring. Build to 15-20 minutes over weeks.
- **Stop if dissociating:** If the client feels disconnected, spacey, or "not in their body," straighten the legs and stop the tremoring. Ground with 5-4-3-2-1 or orienting.
- **Not for acute psychosis:** TRE can release intense emotions and body memories. It requires a baseline level of psychological stability.
- **Self-regulation:** The tremoring can be stopped at any time by straightening the legs or changing position. Clients should know they are in control.
- **Post-TRE:** Rest for 5-10 minutes. Drink water. Notice how the body feels. Some people feel energized; others feel tired. Both are normal.
- **Professional guidance:** While TRE is designed for self-practice, the first 2-3 sessions should be guided by a certified TRE provider, especially for trauma survivors.

## Body Awareness Exercises

### Interoceptive Awareness

Interoception — the sense of the body's internal state — is often disrupted in trauma, anxiety, alexithymia (difficulty identifying emotions), and eating disorders. Building interoceptive awareness is foundational for all somatic work.

**Practice:**
1. Sit quietly and close your eyes
2. Bring attention to the interior of the body — not the surface, but the inside
3. Start with gross sensations: heartbeat, breathing, temperature
4. Gradually notice subtler signals: gut feelings, muscle tension patterns, energy level, hunger/fullness, the felt sense of emotions
5. Practice noticing without judging or interpreting — simply "What is present?"

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes. Build gradually. Clients with trauma or dissociation may find this challenging — start with external sensory awareness and work inward.

### Body Mapping

A creative, externalized way to connect emotions and body sensations.

**Instructions:**
1. Draw (or use a printed outline of) a body — front and back views
2. Using colors, patterns, or symbols, mark where you feel different emotions or sensations
3. Common associations: anger as red in the chest or fists, anxiety as yellow or jagged lines in the stomach, sadness as blue/heavy in the chest, shame as dark in the face and gut, calm as green or open in the belly

**Clinical use:** Body maps can be done at the start and end of treatment to track changes in somatic experience. They can be done session-to-session to explore a specific emotional state. They bypass verbal defenses and give concrete form to felt experience.

### Sensation Vocabulary

Many clients lack words for body sensations, limiting their ability to communicate and process somatic experience. Building a sensation vocabulary is a prerequisite for deeper somatic work.

**Sensation word list:**

| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| Temperature | Warm, hot, cool, cold, burning, icy, flushed |
| Pressure | Heavy, light, pressing, squeezing, expanding, compressed, full, empty |
| Movement | Pulsing, vibrating, buzzing, trembling, flowing, still, stuck, spreading, contracting, radiating, rising, sinking |
| Texture | Sharp, dull, smooth, rough, jagged, soft, hard, tight, loose |
| Size/Shape | Big, small, round, pointed, dense, diffuse, contained, boundless |
| Quality | Electric, tingling, numb, achy, throbbing, itchy, floating, grounded, open, closed, spacious, constricted |

**Practice:** At the start of each session, ask the client to scan their body and describe what they notice using sensation words. Offer the vocabulary list as a reference. Over time, clients develop a richer and more precise language for body experience, which is itself therapeutic — naming a sensation creates a degree of separation from it and makes it more manageable.

### When to Use Somatic Practices

| Presentation | Recommended Practices |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Acute panic/anxiety | Cold water (dive reflex), extended exhale, butterfly hug, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding |
| Chronic hyperarousal/hypervigilance | Coherent breathing, PMR, vagal toning (humming, gargling), bilateral stimulation |
| Dissociation/numbness/hypoarousal | Orienting, strong sensory input, movement, firm touch, social engagement |
| Traumatic activation (flashback) | Grounding, orientation, pendulation (resource → activation → resource), bilateral stimulation |
| Stored muscle tension | TRE, completing defensive responses, body scan, PMR |
| Alexithymia/poor interoception | Body mapping, sensation vocabulary, interoceptive awareness practice |
| Chronic stress/burnout | Diaphragmatic breathing, coherent breathing, TRE, self-compassion body scan |
| Sleep difficulties | 4-7-8 breathing, extended exhale, body scan, PMR |
