# crisis-intervention

This skill provides structured frameworks for assessing and responding to acute psychological crises. It supplements — never replaces — professional training, supervision, and institutional protocols.

**⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER:** This is reference material for qualified mental health professionals. In acute crisis situations, always follow your professional training, institutional protocols, and local emergency procedures. If there is imminent danger to life, contact emergency services immediately. This tool does not provide real-time crisis intervention.

## Suicide risk assessment — Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)

The C-SSRS (Posner et al., 2011) is the most widely used structured suicide assessment tool. Recommended by FDA, WHO, CDC, VA/DoD, and adopted in 45+ countries. It distinguishes ideation from behavior and tracks severity over time.

### Ideation severity (5 levels)

Each level represents increasing severity. Ask questions verbatim or adapted naturally:

| Level | Category | Screening question |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Wish to be dead | "Have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?" |
| **2** | Non-specific active ideation | "Have you actually had any thoughts of killing yourself?" |
| **3** | Active ideation with any methods (not plan) | "Have you been thinking about how you might do this?" |
| **4** | Active ideation with some intent, without specific plan | "Have you had these thoughts and had some intention of acting on them?" |
| **5** | Active ideation with specific plan and intent | "Have you started to work out or worked out the details of how to kill yourself? Do you intend to carry out this plan?" |

If any level ≥ 2: proceed to ideation intensity assessment.

### Ideation intensity (for most severe ideation endorsed)

| Dimension | Assessment | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | How often do you have these thoughts? | 1 = less than once/week … 5 = many times daily |
| **Duration** | How long do they last? | 1 = fleeting seconds … 5 = continuous/sustained |
| **Controllability** | Can you stop thinking about it? | 0 = easily … 5 = no control at all |
| **Deterrents** | Is anything stopping you? | 0 = definite deterrents … 5 = nothing stops me |
| **Reasons** | Why do you have these thoughts? | To get attention/relief vs. to end life |

### Suicidal behavior assessment

| Behavior | Definition | Key questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Actual attempt** | Self-injurious behavior with at least some intent to die | "Have you done anything to try to kill yourself?" |
| **Interrupted attempt** | Interrupted by external circumstances before completion | "Has anyone stopped you from trying to kill yourself?" |
| **Aborted attempt** | Self-stopped before beginning the act | "Have you started to do something to end your life but stopped yourself?" |
| **Preparatory acts** | Steps toward an attempt (e.g., acquiring means, writing a note, giving away possessions) | "Have you done anything to prepare to end your life?" |
| **Non-suicidal self-injury** | Self-harm without intent to die | Differentiate: "When you did X, did you intend to die?" |

### Risk factors

**Static (historical — cannot change):**
- Prior suicide attempt(s) — **strongest single predictor**
- Family history of suicide
- History of trauma, abuse, or neglect
- Chronic pain or terminal illness
- History of psychiatric hospitalization

**Dynamic (current — can change):**
- Current substance use or intoxication
- Recent significant loss (relationship, job, financial, bereavement)
- Social isolation / living alone
- Agitation, insomnia, severe anxiety
- Hopelessness (Beck Hopelessness Scale)
- Access to lethal means (especially firearms)
- Recent discharge from psychiatric hospital (first 90 days = highest risk period)
- Non-adherence to treatment
- Current legal or disciplinary problems
- Exposure to another's suicide (contagion)

### Protective factors

- Reasons for living (assess directly: "What keeps you going?")
- Social support and connectedness
- Responsibility to children or others who depend on them
- Treatment engagement and therapeutic alliance
- Problem-solving ability
- Religious/moral/cultural objections to suicide
- Fear of pain or disfigurement from suicide
- Pets (documented protective factor)
- Future orientation (plans, goals, events to look forward to)

### Risk level classification

| Level | Criteria | Recommended actions |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Passive ideation only (Level 1), no plan or intent, multiple protective factors, no prior attempts | Document assessment. Continue monitoring. Review at every session. Educate on warning signs. |
| **Moderate** | Active ideation without plan (Levels 2-3), OR passive with few protective factors, OR remote history of attempt | Increase session frequency. Complete safety plan. Consider involving support system. Consult supervisor. Document thoroughly. |
| **High** | Active ideation with plan but no current intent (Level 4), OR recent aborted/interrupted attempt, OR multiple risk factors with few protective | Immediate clinical intervention. Activate safety plan. Contact support system with consent. Consider voluntary hospitalization. Notify supervisor. Restrict access to means. Daily or next-day follow-up. |
| **Imminent** | Active ideation with plan and intent (Level 5), OR actual attempt (recent), OR command hallucinations to self-harm | **Call emergency services.** Do not leave person alone. Involuntary psychiatric hold if necessary. Accompany to emergency department. Ensure continuous monitoring. |

## Safety plan — Stanley-Brown model (Stanley & Brown, 2012)

The Stanley-Brown safety plan is the evidence-based standard for suicide prevention planning. It replaces the discredited "no-suicide contract." Research shows safety plans reduce suicide attempts by approximately 50% (Stanley et al., 2018).

### Six steps

**Step 1 — Warning signs**
Specific internal experiences that signal a crisis is developing:
- Thoughts: "Things would be better without me," "I can't take this anymore"
- Images: seeing yourself dead, imagining the method
- Moods: sudden emptiness, overwhelming despair, intense anger
- Situations: being alone at night, after conflict, anniversary of loss
- Behaviors: withdrawing, drinking more, giving things away, researching methods

*Help the client be specific — "feeling bad" is too vague. "That hollow feeling in my chest after a fight with my partner" is actionable.*

**Step 2 — Internal coping strategies**
Things the person can do alone, without contacting anyone, to distract or self-soothe:
- Exercise (even a walk)
- Listen to specific music
- Deep breathing or grounding exercises
- Take a shower (alternate hot/cold)
- Write in journal
- Play with pet
- Cook or bake
- Watch a specific comforting show
- Clean or organize

*List at least 3-5 specific strategies. Test them: "Which of these could you actually do when you're in that dark place?"*

**Step 3 — People and social settings for distraction**
Friends, family, or places that provide social contact and distraction (without needing to disclose the crisis):
- Name: [name] | Phone: [number] — "I can call them to chat about anything"
- Places: coffee shop, gym, library, place of worship, park

*Not crisis contacts — just normal social connection to break isolation.*

**Step 4 — People I can ask for help**
Trusted individuals the person can tell they're struggling:
- Name: [name] | Phone: [number] | What to say: "I'm having a hard time and need to talk"
- Name: [name] | Phone: [number]

*At least 2-3 people. Rehearse what to say: "I need help right now" or "Can I come over? I'm not doing well."*

**Step 5 — Professionals and agencies to contact in crisis**
- Therapist: [name] | Phone: [number] | After-hours: [number/instructions]
- Psychiatrist: [name] | Phone: [number]
- Crisis line: [local crisis line number]
- Crisis text line: [local text line]
- Emergency services: [local emergency number]
- Nearest emergency department: [name and address]

**Step 6 — Making the environment safe**
Reducing access to lethal means:
- Firearms: remove from home, lock in safe, give to trusted person, trigger locks
- Medications: limit quantities, give excess to someone else, use pill lockbox
- Sharp objects: secure or remove
- Other means: address based on client's specific plan

*Means restriction is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies. Discuss openly and non-judgmentally.*

**Additional:**
- "The one thing that is most important to me and worth living for is: _______________"

### Safety plan best practices

- Complete collaboratively during a calm moment — not during active crisis
- Write on a physical card AND save in phone
- Review and update regularly (especially after a crisis or change in circumstances)
- Practice using it: "Let's walk through what happens when you notice [warning sign from Step 1]"
- Give copy to a trusted person from Step 4

## Self-harm assessment

Self-harm and suicidal behavior are distinct but overlapping phenomena. Always assess both.

### Function analysis

| Function | Description | Indicators | Alternatives |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Emotion regulation** | Reducing overwhelming affect — "it makes the pain stop" | Precedes emotional numbness, done privately, tension release | Ice cube on skin, red marker, intense exercise, cold shower, snapping rubber band |
| **Communication** | Expressing distress to others — "so they'll know how much I'm hurting" | Done where others will see, follows interpersonal conflict | "I need help" scripts, assertiveness training, DEAR MAN (DBT) |
| **Self-punishment** | Punishing self for perceived failings | Accompanies shame/guilt, harsh self-talk | Compassion-focused exercises, cognitive restructuring of self-blame |
| **Dissociation management** | "Feeling something" to counter numbness | Accompanies depersonalization/derealization, detached affect | Strong sensory input (ice, strong taste), grounding exercises, movement |
| **Sensation seeking** | Adrenaline/endorphin rush | Risk-taking patterns, thrill-seeking | Intense exercise, cold water immersion, spicy food |

### Assessment dimensions

- **Method:** cutting, burning, hitting, hair pulling, other
- **Frequency:** how often (daily, weekly, monthly)
- **Severity:** superficial vs requiring medical attention, escalation over time
- **Concurrent suicidality:** self-harm ≠ suicide attempt, but they co-occur — always ask about intent to die
- **Triggers:** what happens immediately before
- **Aftermath:** what happens after (relief? shame? more distress?)

## De-escalation techniques

### Verbal de-escalation principles

1. **Be empathic and non-judgmental** — validate feelings without agreeing with harmful actions
2. **Listen actively** — reflect back what you hear, demonstrate understanding
3. **Acknowledge their feelings** — "I can see you're in a lot of pain right now"
4. **Use a calm, measured tone** — match their volume slightly below theirs, slow your speech
5. **Avoid confrontation** — don't argue, correct, or challenge during acute distress
6. **Offer choices** — restore sense of control: "Would you like to sit or stand? Water or tea?"
7. **Set clear, gentle limits** — "I want to help you. I need you to stay safe so we can talk."
8. **Be honest** — don't make promises you can't keep
9. **Allow silence** — don't rush to fill space. Silence can be calming.
10. **Maintain safe physical positioning** — respect personal space, don't block exits, position yourself at an angle rather than face-to-face

### Grounding during acute distress

**5-4-3-2-1 technique:**
- Name 5 things you can see
- Name 4 things you can touch/feel
- Name 3 things you can hear
- Name 2 things you can smell
- Name 1 thing you can taste

**Feet on the floor:**
- Press feet firmly into the ground
- Notice the pressure, the temperature, the texture
- "You are here. You are safe in this room. Feel the floor holding you up."

**Cold water:**
- Splash cold water on face (activates dive reflex, drops heart rate)
- Hold ice cubes in hands
- Cold compress on back of neck

**Name 3 things:**
- 3 things you can see in this room
- 3 colors you notice
- 3 sounds you can hear right now

### Containing overwhelming affect

- **Paced breathing:** In for 4, out for 6-8. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system.
- **Butterfly hug:** Cross arms over chest, alternate tapping shoulders. Bilateral stimulation for self-regulation.
- **Safe place imagery:** Guide to a remembered or imagined safe place — describe in sensory detail
- **Containment imagery:** "Imagine putting this distress in a strong container — a box, a vault, a safe. You can close it and come back to it when you're ready, with support."

## Mandated reporting

**Note:** Reporting requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction. Clinicians must know their local laws. This section provides general principles only.

### General principles

- **Who must report:** Mental health professionals are typically mandated reporters for suspected abuse/neglect of children, elders, and dependent adults
- **Standard:** Reasonable suspicion — you do NOT need to confirm abuse. If you suspect it, you report it.
- **Report to:** Child protective services, adult protective services, and/or law enforcement (varies by jurisdiction)
- **Timing:** Typically immediately or within 24-48 hours of learning of suspected abuse
- **Confidentiality:** Mandated reporting is a legal exception to confidentiality. Inform clients of this limit during informed consent at treatment outset.
- **Do not investigate** — that is the role of protective services. Report what was observed/disclosed.

### Documentation

- What was observed or disclosed (client's exact words when possible)
- What was reported, to whom, when, report reference number
- Any response from the reporting agency
- Clinical rationale for the report

### Therapeutic relationship

- Reporting can damage the therapeutic alliance. When possible, inform the client before reporting.
- Frame as: "I care about safety, and the law requires me to share this information so that people who can help can get involved."
- Continue therapy if possible — the alliance may recover.

## Post-crisis follow-up

### Immediate follow-up (24-48 hours)

- Contact the client to check on their status
- Review: is the safety plan accessible? Did they use it? Did it help?
- Assess current risk level — has it changed?
- Confirm next appointment

### Treatment adjustments

- Increase session frequency (e.g., twice weekly during high-risk periods)
- Add stabilization techniques to treatment plan
- Consider medication evaluation referral if not already on psychotropics
- Add crisis-specific interventions (DBT distress tolerance, safety planning updates)
- Consider higher level of care if outpatient is insufficient (IOP, PHP, inpatient)

### Documentation requirements

Document thoroughly:
- Risk assessment findings (C-SSRS levels, risk/protective factors)
- Clinical decision-making rationale (why this risk level, why this intervention)
- Interventions implemented (safety plan, means restriction, contacts made)
- Follow-up plan (next contact, frequency increase, referrals)
- Consultations (supervisor, colleague — who, when, recommendations)
- Client's response to interventions

### Supervision and self-care

- Consult with supervisor or colleague after every high-risk assessment
- Document the consultation
- Acknowledge the emotional impact on yourself — working with suicidal clients is one of the most stressful aspects of clinical work
- Use peer support, personal therapy, and professional development

## Common mistakes

- **Not asking directly about suicide.** Asking does NOT increase risk — research consistently shows this (Dazzi et al., 2014). Not asking leaves you blind.
- **Using "no-suicide contracts."** These have no evidence base and may provide false reassurance. Use safety plans instead.
- **Not assessing access to means.** Means restriction is among the most effective prevention strategies.
- **Not involving the support system.** Isolation is a risk factor. Involving trusted others (with consent when possible) saves lives.
- **Relying on clinical intuition alone.** Use structured assessment (C-SSRS). Intuition misses cases that structured tools catch.
- **Not documenting risk assessment.** Document every risk assessment — the reasoning, the risk level, the plan.
- **Not following up after crisis.** The post-crisis period (especially first week after discharge or crisis) is the highest-risk period.
- **Pathologizing all self-harm as suicidal.** Self-harm and suicide are related but distinct. Assess function and intent separately.
- **"Contracting for safety" instead of safety planning.** A contract is a piece of paper. A safety plan is a clinical intervention.
- **Assuming the crisis has passed because the client "seems better."** Paradoxical improvement can occur when someone has made a decision to act — they feel relieved. Continue monitoring.
