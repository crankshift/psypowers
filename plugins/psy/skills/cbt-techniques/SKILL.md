# cbt-techniques

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the most extensively researched psychotherapeutic modality, with robust evidence for depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, OCD, eating disorders, insomnia, chronic pain, and substance use. The core model holds that psychological distress is maintained by the reciprocal interaction of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors: distorted or unhelpful cognitions drive negative emotions, which in turn drive maladaptive behaviors, which reinforce the cognitions. CBT intervenes at each node of this cycle. Treatment is typically structured, time-limited (12-20 sessions), collaborative, and present-focused, with heavy emphasis on between-session homework.

## Cognitive Techniques

### Automatic Thought Identification

Automatic thoughts are the rapid, often unnoticed cognitions that arise in response to situations. They feel self-evidently true and typically carry a strong emotional charge. The first step in CBT is teaching clients to catch these "hot thoughts."

**How to identify automatic thoughts:**
- Ask: "What was going through your mind just then?" immediately after noticing an emotion shift
- Look for the thought closest to the emotion peak — the "hot thought"
- Automatic thoughts are often brief, telegraphic ("I'm going to fail," "She hates me," "This is hopeless")
- They may appear as images rather than words (e.g., a mental image of being laughed at)

**Thought monitoring:** Assign daily logging of situations, emotions (with intensity 0-100), and the automatic thought connected to each. Even 3-5 entries per day builds awareness rapidly. Clients often report that the act of monitoring alone reduces the grip of automatic thoughts.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Confusing thoughts with emotions ("I felt like a failure" is a thought, not a feeling)
- Capturing surface thoughts rather than the hot thought
- Clients reporting what they think they "should" have been thinking

### Thought Records (7-Column Format)

The thought record is the workhorse of cognitive restructuring. The full 7-column version:

| Column | Prompt | Example |
|--------|--------|---------|
| 1. Situation | What happened? Where? When? Who was there? | "Monday 3pm, boss asked me to redo the report in the team meeting" |
| 2. Emotions | What did you feel? Rate intensity 0-100 | Shame (85), Anger (40), Anxiety (70) |
| 3. Automatic Thought | What went through your mind? Circle the hot thought | "I'm incompetent. Everyone saw. I'll never get promoted." Hot thought: "I'm incompetent" |
| 4. Evidence For | What facts support the hot thought? | "I did make errors in the report. Boss pointed them out publicly." |
| 5. Evidence Against | What facts contradict the hot thought? | "I've had positive reviews. I completed the Johnson project successfully. Everyone makes errors. Boss asks many people to revise." |
| 6. Balanced Thought | What's a more realistic way to see this? | "I made mistakes on this report, which is disappointing but normal. It doesn't mean I'm incompetent overall. I can learn from the feedback." |
| 7. Outcome | Re-rate emotions. What will you do? | Shame (35), Anger (20), Anxiety (30). Plan: fix the report, ask boss for private feedback next time. |

**Implementation tips:**
- Start with a 3-column version (situation, thought, emotion) for the first 1-2 weeks
- Expand to 5 columns (add evidence for/against) once monitoring is fluent
- Add balanced thought and outcome columns when the client can generate alternative perspectives
- Do the first several records collaboratively in session before assigning as homework
- Physical paper records often work better than apps — the act of writing slows down the cognitive process

### Cognitive Distortions Catalog

Cognitive distortions are systematic errors in thinking that maintain negative beliefs. Teach clients to identify which patterns are most active for them.

**All-or-nothing thinking (black-and-white thinking):** Seeing things in absolute categories with no middle ground. "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure." Look for words like "always," "never," "completely," "totally."

**Overgeneralization:** Drawing a sweeping conclusion from a single event. "I got rejected — no one will ever want me." Marker: one negative event becomes a never-ending pattern.

**Mental filter (selective abstraction):** Focusing exclusively on a single negative detail while ignoring the broader positive context. Getting 9 positive reviews and 1 negative, and dwelling only on the negative.

**Disqualifying the positive:** Actively dismissing positive experiences as not counting. "They only said that to be nice." Transforms neutral or positive evidence into negative.

**Jumping to conclusions:** Making negative interpretations without evidence.
- *Mind reading:* Assuming you know what others think. "She thinks I'm boring."
- *Fortune telling:* Predicting negative outcomes as certainties. "I know the presentation will go badly."

**Magnification/minimization (binocular trick):** Exaggerating the importance of negatives or shrinking the significance of positives. Magnifying a mistake while minimizing an achievement.

**Emotional reasoning:** Taking feelings as proof of reality. "I feel anxious, so something bad must be about to happen." "I feel guilty, so I must have done something wrong."

**Should statements:** Rigid rules about how self/others/world must be. "I should always be productive." "People should be fair." Creates guilt (self-directed shoulds) or anger (other-directed shoulds). Also watch for "must," "ought to," "have to."

**Labeling:** Attaching a fixed, global label to self or others based on a single event. "I'm a loser" instead of "I made an error." An extreme form of overgeneralization.

**Personalization:** Assuming excessive responsibility for external events. "The team failed because of me." Taking things personally that have nothing to do with you.

**Catastrophizing:** Jumping to the worst possible outcome and treating it as probable. "If I make this mistake, I'll be fired, become homeless, and my life will be over."

**Control fallacies:** Two forms — feeling externally controlled ("I can't help it, life controls me") or internally controlling everything ("If anyone is unhappy, it's my fault").

**Fallacy of fairness:** Believing that life should be fair and feeling resentful when it is not. "It's not fair that she got promoted and I didn't."

**Blaming:** Holding others entirely responsible for your emotional pain, or conversely, blaming yourself for every problem. Opposite of personalization when directed outward.

**Always being right:** The need to prove that one's opinions and actions are correct. Being wrong is unthinkable. Will go to any length to demonstrate rightness.

### Socratic Questioning

Socratic questioning is the primary method for examining automatic thoughts. Rather than directly challenging thoughts (which creates resistance), use guided discovery to help the client evaluate the thought themselves.

**The 5 key Socratic questions:**

1. **"What's the evidence for this thought? What's the evidence against it?"** — Grounds evaluation in facts rather than feelings. Often the evidence against is substantial but has been filtered out.

2. **"Is there an alternative explanation?"** — Opens up other ways to interpret the situation. "What else could explain what happened?"

3. **"What's the worst that could happen? The best? The most realistic?"** — Decatastrophizes. Often the worst case is survivable, the best case is more positive than expected, and the most realistic case is manageable.

4. **"What would I tell a friend in this situation?"** — Bypasses self-critical bias. Clients are almost always more compassionate and balanced when advising others.

5. **"What's the effect of thinking this way? What would be the effect of changing my thinking?"** — Shifts focus to the pragmatic consequences of the belief, regardless of its truth value.

**Technique:** Use a curious, non-confrontational tone. You are genuinely exploring together, not cross-examining. Follow the client's reasoning. Let them arrive at the conclusion.

### Downward Arrow Technique

Used to uncover intermediate beliefs and core beliefs that underlie automatic thoughts. Starting from a surface-level automatic thought, repeatedly ask: "If that were true, what would that mean about you/your life/the world?"

**Example chain:**
- "I failed the exam" → "What does that mean to you?"
- "That I'm not smart enough" → "And if that were true, what would that mean?"
- "That I'll never succeed in my career" → "And what would that mean about you?"
- "That I'm fundamentally inadequate" ← **Core belief reached**

Core beliefs tend to fall into three categories:
- **Helplessness:** "I am incompetent / powerless / vulnerable / trapped"
- **Unlovability:** "I am unlovable / unwanted / bound to be rejected"
- **Worthlessness:** "I am worthless / defective / not good enough"

Once identified, core beliefs can be addressed through behavioral experiments, positive data logs, and continuum techniques.

### Behavioral Experiments

Testing predictions directly through experience. More powerful than purely verbal restructuring because they generate new evidence.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the prediction (automatic thought or belief being tested)
2. Rate belief strength (0-100%)
3. Design an experiment to test the prediction
4. Predict the outcome specifically and measurably
5. Carry out the experiment
6. Record what actually happened
7. Evaluate: what did you learn? Re-rate belief strength

**Example:** Belief: "If I speak up in the meeting, everyone will think I'm stupid" (belief: 80%). Experiment: share one idea in the next team meeting. Prediction: people will laugh or dismiss me. Actual outcome: two colleagues built on the idea, one disagreed respectfully. Re-rated belief: 35%.

**Types of behavioral experiments:**
- *Discovery experiments:* "Let me find out what happens when..."
- *Hypothesis testing:* "I predict X — let's see if that's accurate"
- *Surveys:* Asking others to test assumptions about what people think

### Cost-Benefit Analysis

Evaluating the advantages and disadvantages of a belief or behavior. Useful when a client is intellectually aware a belief is distorted but still clings to it.

**Format:** Draw a 2x2 grid. List advantages and disadvantages of holding the belief, then advantages and disadvantages of letting it go. Weight each item by importance. Often reveals that the costs of maintaining the belief far outweigh the benefits.

## Behavioral Techniques

### Behavioral Activation

The primary behavioral intervention for depression. Based on the principle that depression leads to withdrawal, which leads to loss of positive reinforcement, which deepens depression.

**Activity monitoring (week 1-2):**
- Track hourly activities for 1 week using a daily schedule
- Rate each activity for Mastery (sense of achievement, 0-10) and Pleasure (enjoyment, 0-10)
- Identifies patterns: what is being avoided? What provides even small doses of mastery or pleasure?

**Pleasant activity scheduling (week 2+):**
- From monitoring data, identify activities with higher mastery/pleasure ratings
- Schedule 1-2 additional pleasant or mastery activities per day
- Start small — "take a 10-minute walk" not "start going to the gym 5 days a week"
- Graded task assignment: break overwhelming tasks into the smallest possible first step

**Key principles:**
- Act first, motivation follows (not the other way around)
- Schedule activities — don't wait until you "feel like it"
- Outside-in approach: changing behavior changes mood, which changes thoughts
- Monitor mood before and after activities to demonstrate the connection

### Activity Scheduling

A weekly template that balances three types of activity:
- **Routine:** necessary life maintenance (meals, hygiene, commute, chores)
- **Pleasure:** activities done purely for enjoyment (hobbies, socializing, entertainment)
- **Mastery:** activities that give a sense of accomplishment (work tasks, learning, exercise)

Depression typically collapses all three into routine-only. The goal is restoring balance. Use a weekly grid (days x hourly blocks) and pre-schedule at least one pleasure and one mastery activity per day.

## Exposure Techniques

### Exposure Hierarchy Construction

Exposure is the gold-standard treatment for anxiety disorders. The hierarchy is a graded list of feared situations or stimuli, ranked by Subjective Units of Distress (SUDS).

**SUDS anchoring (0-100):**
- 0 = completely calm, no anxiety
- 25 = mild discomfort, easily manageable
- 50 = moderate anxiety, uncomfortable but tolerable
- 75 = high anxiety, very difficult, strong urge to escape
- 100 = worst anxiety imaginable, panic-level

**Building the hierarchy (10-15 items):**
1. Identify the fear theme (e.g., social evaluation, contamination, heights)
2. Brainstorm all related situations the client avoids or endures with distress
3. Rate each item with a SUDS score
4. Arrange from lowest to highest
5. Ensure items are spaced roughly evenly (no jumps of more than 10-15 SUDS)
6. Fill gaps with intermediate items
7. Start exposure at the lowest item the client is willing to attempt (typically SUDS 25-40)

### Types of Exposure

**In-vivo exposure:** Direct, real-life contact with the feared stimulus. Most effective. Example: for social anxiety, ordering coffee while making small talk; for contamination OCD, touching a doorknob and delaying hand washing.

**Imaginal exposure:** Vivid mental imagery of the feared situation. Used when in-vivo is impractical (trauma memories, feared catastrophes), or as a stepping stone. Write detailed scripts in present tense with sensory details. Read aloud or listen to recordings.

**Interoceptive exposure:** Deliberately inducing feared physical sensations. Used primarily for panic disorder. Examples: spinning in a chair (dizziness), breathing through a straw (air hunger), running in place (rapid heartbeat), hyperventilating (lightheadedness).

### Habituation Principles and Session Structure

**Within-session habituation:** Anxiety rises initially, then naturally declines if the person stays in the situation without avoidance. Sessions should be long enough for meaningful SUDS reduction (typically 30-90 minutes per exposure item).

**Between-session habituation:** With repeated exposures, the initial SUDS peak decreases over sessions.

**Session structure:**
1. Review the hierarchy and select the target item
2. Discuss what to expect (anxiety will rise, then fall)
3. Rate pre-exposure SUDS
4. Begin exposure — remain in the situation
5. Rate SUDS every 5 minutes
6. Continue until SUDS drops by at least 50% or below 25
7. Do not end during peak anxiety — this reinforces avoidance
8. Debrief: what happened vs. what you predicted?
9. Assign repeated practice between sessions

**Key rule:** No safety behaviors during exposure (checking, reassurance-seeking, distraction, carrying medications "just in case"). Safety behaviors prevent learning.

## Relapse Prevention

Relapse prevention is built into the final 2-3 sessions of CBT.

**Components:**
1. **Identify warning signs:** What are the early indicators of relapse? (specific thoughts, behaviors, mood shifts, sleep changes, social withdrawal)
2. **Create a coping plan:** A written document listing (a) personal warning signs, (b) skills learned in therapy, (c) which skill to use for which warning sign, (d) people to contact for support
3. **Anticipate high-risk situations:** Life events, transitions, or stressors that could trigger relapse
4. **Booster sessions:** Schedule 1-3 follow-up sessions at 1, 3, and 6 months post-treatment
5. **Therapy blueprint:** A one-page summary of what was learned in therapy — key beliefs identified, most helpful techniques, evidence against core beliefs — to review when needed

**Homework template for relapse prevention:**
- "What did I learn in therapy?" (3-5 key insights)
- "What techniques helped me most?" (rank top 3)
- "What are my early warning signs?" (list 5-7)
- "What is my action plan when I notice warning signs?" (step-by-step)
- "Who will I reach out to?" (2-3 contacts)
