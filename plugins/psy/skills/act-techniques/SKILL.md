# act-techniques

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT, pronounced as the word "act") aims to increase psychological flexibility — the ability to contact the present moment fully as a conscious human being, and to change or persist in behavior when doing so serves valued ends. Developed by Steven Hayes in the 1980s, ACT is rooted in Relational Frame Theory (RFT), a behavioral account of human language and cognition. ACT does not aim to reduce symptoms directly — instead, it changes the relationship to symptoms. The core insight: it is not the content of thoughts and feelings that causes suffering, but the way we relate to that content (fusion, avoidance, disconnection from values). ACT organizes its processes in the "hexaflex" model — six interconnected processes arranged in a hexagon, with psychological flexibility at the center.

## Process 1 -- Acceptance (vs. Experiential Avoidance)

Experiential avoidance — the attempt to escape, avoid, or suppress unwanted internal experiences (thoughts, feelings, sensations, memories) — is a central driver of psychopathology in the ACT model. Acceptance is the alternative: actively embracing internal experience without trying to change its form or frequency.

### Willingness Exercises

**Willingness scale (0-10):** "On a scale of 0 to 10, how willing are you to have this feeling/thought and still do what matters? 0 means you would do anything to avoid it. 10 means you would welcome it fully." This distinguishes willingness (a behavioral stance) from wanting (an emotional preference). You can be willing to experience anxiety without wanting to feel anxious.

**"Opening up" vs. "closing down":** Invite the client to notice their posture and energy when they resist an experience (tight, contracted, bracing) vs. when they allow it (open, expansive, soft). Practice literally opening the body — uncrossing arms, softening the belly, breathing into the chest — as a physical anchor for willingness.

### Clean vs. Dirty Discomfort

- **Clean discomfort (primary pain):** The natural, unavoidable pain of being human — grief after loss, anxiety before a challenge, frustration when blocked. It hurts, but it passes.
- **Dirty discomfort (secondary suffering):** The suffering added by struggling against clean discomfort — anxiety about anxiety, anger at sadness, shame about fear. This layer is optional and is maintained by experiential avoidance.

The ACT goal is not to eliminate clean discomfort but to reduce dirty discomfort by dropping the struggle.

### Key Metaphors for Acceptance

**Struggle switch metaphor:** Imagine that behind your pain there's a switch labeled "struggle." When the switch is ON, you fight the pain — and the fight amplifies it. When you turn the switch OFF, the pain is still there, but the amplification stops. You can't turn off pain, but you can turn off the struggle with pain.

**Quicksand metaphor:** When you step into quicksand, every instinct says to struggle — but struggling makes you sink faster. The counterintuitive solution is to spread out, make full contact with the surface, and lie still. Acceptance is like that — making full contact with the experience instead of thrashing against it.

**Chinese finger trap:** The harder you pull to escape, the tighter it grips. The solution is to push in — to move toward the experience rather than away from it.

### Expansion Technique

A structured exercise for making room for difficult feelings:

1. **Observe** the feeling in your body. Where do you feel it? What are its qualities? (heavy, tight, sharp, warm, pulsing)
2. **Breathe into** the sensation. Imagine your breath flowing into and around it, creating space.
3. **Make room.** Instead of tensing against the sensation, soften around it. Imagine the sensation has more space to exist without being squeezed.
4. **Allow** the feeling to be there. You don't have to like it, want it, or approve of it. Just let it be. Say internally: "I don't like this feeling, but I can make room for it."
5. **Notice** that you are larger than the feeling. It is in you, but it is not all of you.

## Process 2 -- Cognitive Defusion (vs. Fusion)

Cognitive fusion is the tendency to get entangled with thought content — to treat thoughts as literal truths, commands to obey, or threats to respond to. Defusion does not change thought content; it changes the relationship to thoughts, creating distance and reducing their behavioral impact.

### Defusion Techniques

**"I'm having the thought that...":** The simplest and most widely used defusion technique. Instead of "I'm a failure," say "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure." Then extend: "I notice I'm having the thought that I'm a failure." Each layer adds distance. The thought content is identical — but the relationship to it shifts from "this is reality" to "this is a mental event I'm observing."

**Leaves on a stream:** Close your eyes and imagine sitting beside a gently flowing stream with leaves floating on the surface. As each thought arises, place it on a leaf and watch it drift downstream. Don't try to speed the leaves up, slow them down, or change what's on them. If you get caught up in a thought, notice that you've left the bank of the stream — and gently return. Practice for 5-10 minutes.

**Naming the story:** When a familiar pattern of negative thinking emerges, give it a name. "Ah, there's the 'I'm not good enough' story again." Or "Here comes the 'nobody likes me' narrative." Naming externalizes the pattern and makes it recognizable rather than believable. Over time, clients can greet recurring stories with familiarity rather than alarm: "Oh, I know this one."

**Thanking your mind:** When the mind produces a particularly harsh or catastrophic thought, respond with: "Thanks, mind. Very creative." Or "Appreciate the input, mind." This acknowledges the mind's attempt to protect you while declining to be governed by it. It introduces lightness without dismissiveness.

**Silly voices technique:** Take the distressing thought and repeat it in a cartoon voice — Mickey Mouse, a movie villain, a sports announcer. The content stays the same, but fusion breaks because the thought becomes absurd. "I'm worthless" said in Daffy Duck's voice is hard to take literally. Use with care — some clients may experience this as trivializing their pain. Frame it as an experiment in how context changes the impact of words.

**Word repetition (Titchener effect):** Take a single distressing word (e.g., "failure," "stupid," "worthless") and repeat it aloud rapidly for 30-45 seconds. The word loses its meaning and becomes just a sound — a sequence of phonemes. This demonstrates experientially that words are sounds we have agreed to attach meaning to, not inherent truths. This can be done with any word — start with a neutral word ("milk") to demonstrate the effect, then apply it to the target word.

**Thought as object:** "If this thought had a color, what would it be? A shape? A size? A texture? A weight? A temperature? How fast is it moving?" Externalizing the thought as a physical object creates separation and often reveals information about the thought's emotional charge.

### When to Use Defusion

Defusion is indicated when:
- The client is rigid, stuck, or repetitively caught in thought loops
- Cognitive restructuring has failed or feels like arguing with thoughts
- The thought is not factually wrong — it may even be true — but fusion with it is driving avoidance or inaction
- The client says things like "I know it's irrational, but I can't stop thinking it"

Defusion is not about proving thoughts wrong (that's cognitive restructuring). It's about changing the function of thoughts — from rules that govern behavior to passing mental events.

## Process 3 -- Present Moment Awareness (vs. Past/Future Focus)

Contact with the present moment means showing up to your life as it is happening now, rather than being pulled into rumination (past) or worry (future). This is the ACT version of mindfulness — pragmatic, values-connected, and focused on workability.

### Present Moment Exercises

**Mindful breathing (2-5 min):** Attention anchored to the breath at the nostrils, chest, or belly. No need to change the breath — just notice it. When the mind wanders (it will), notice where it went, and gently return. Every return is a rep — the mental equivalent of a bicep curl.

**Noticing exercises — 5 things you can see, hear, feel:** Name (aloud or silently) five things you can see right now, five things you can hear, and five things you can physically feel (feet on the floor, air on the skin, clothing against the body). This rapidly anchors attention in the present sensory world rather than the conceptual world of thoughts.

**Coming back:** A micro-practice usable at any moment. Simply notice "I've drifted" and choose to come back to what's here, right now. The noticing is the skill — not the staying.

**"Here and now" anchoring during sessions:** When a client is lost in a story about the past or worry about the future, gently bring them back: "What are you experiencing right now, as you tell me this? What do you notice in your body?" This shifts from content to process, from narrative to experience.

## Process 4 -- Self-as-Context (vs. Conceptualized Self)

The conceptualized self is the collection of stories, labels, and self-descriptions we fuse with: "I'm an anxious person," "I'm a failure," "I'm the responsible one." Self-as-context is the observing self — the continuous perspective from which all experience is observed. It is the "you" that has been present through every experience of your life, unchanged by any of them.

### Self-as-Context Exercises

**Observer self exercise:** "There is a part of you that has been there through every experience you've ever had. When you were five, there was a 'you' behind your eyes, looking out. When you were fifteen, that same 'you' was still there — different thoughts, different body, different feelings, but the same observer. That perspective — the you that notices — has never been damaged by anything that has happened to you."

**Chessboard metaphor:** Your thoughts and feelings are the chess pieces — some black, some white, locked in battle. You have been living your life as if you are the pieces, fighting to get the white pieces (good thoughts, positive feelings) to win and eliminate the black pieces (painful thoughts, difficult feelings). But you are not the pieces. You are the board. The board holds all the pieces, is touched by all of them, and is not threatened by any of them. The board doesn't need the white pieces to win.

**Sky and weather metaphor:** You are the sky. Thoughts, feelings, sensations, and memories are the weather — sometimes sunny, sometimes stormy, sometimes a hurricane. The weather changes constantly, but the sky remains. No matter how terrible the storm, it has never damaged the sky. The sky doesn't need to fight the weather. It simply contains it.

**Continuous you exercise:** "Think of a memory from when you were about 10 years old. Picture it. Now notice: you were there. There was a you behind your eyes who saw what happened. Now think of a memory from your teenage years. Notice again: you were there — the same you, the same perspective, even though everything else had changed. Now come back to right here, right now. You are here. The same you who has been present through all of it. Your body has changed, your thoughts have changed, your feelings have changed — but this sense of perspective, of being the one who notices, has remained constant. This is self-as-context."

## Process 5 -- Values (vs. Lack of Direction)

Values in ACT are freely chosen, ongoing directions for living — not goals, not feelings, not rules imposed by others. Values are like a compass direction (west): you can always head west, but you never arrive at west. Goals are landmarks along the way.

### Values Clarification Exercises

**Values card sort:** Present a list of 30-60 values (e.g., connection, adventure, creativity, justice, humor, authenticity, kindness, health, knowledge, loyalty, freedom, courage, contribution, growth, spirituality, family, integrity, leadership, compassion, playfulness). Have the client sort them into "very important," "important," and "not important." Then narrow the "very important" pile to 5-8 core values. Discuss: how actively are you living each one?

**Life compass:** Map values across key life domains:
- Intimate relationships / family
- Friendships / social life
- Work / career
- Education / personal growth
- Recreation / leisure / fun
- Health / physical well-being
- Spirituality / meaning
- Community / citizenship / environment

For each domain: "What kind of person do you want to be in this area? What do you want to stand for? What qualities do you want to bring?"

**Values vs. goals — the critical distinction:**
- Values = directions: "Being a loving, present parent"
- Goals = destinations: "Attend every school event this semester"
- Values are never achieved — they are lived, moment by moment
- Goals are completed — they are steps in a valued direction
- A life organized around goals alone leads to the "arrival fallacy" (achieving the goal and feeling empty). A life organized around values finds meaning in every step.

**Sweet spot exercise:** "If no one would judge you, if you couldn't fail, if money were no object — what would you do with your one life? What would you devote yourself to? What would matter?" This question bypasses social desirability and fear-based avoidance to access genuine values.

**Values-guided action planning:** For each core value, identify:
1. Current actions aligned with this value
2. Current actions misaligned with this value
3. Actions you've been avoiding that would serve this value
4. One small step you could take this week in this valued direction

**Eulogy exercise:** "Imagine your 80th birthday (or your funeral). Someone who knows you deeply stands up to speak. What would you most want them to say about you? Not what they would say — what would you want them to say? What does that tell you about your values?"

## Process 6 -- Committed Action (vs. Inaction/Impulsivity)

Values without action are just pleasant thoughts. Committed action means taking concrete steps in valued directions, even when doing so is difficult, uncomfortable, or frightening. It integrates all the other processes: you accept discomfort, defuse from unhelpful thoughts, stay present, connect with the observing self, and act on your values.

### Committed Action Tools

**SMART goals aligned to values:** Each goal should be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant (to a stated value), and Time-bound. "This week, I will call my mother for 15 minutes on Wednesday evening [specific, measurable, time-bound] because I value family connection [relevant] and this is something I can do even when I feel anxious [achievable]."

**Willingness barriers:** When you identify a valued action and then don't do it, something is showing up to stop you. What is it? Usually it is an unwanted internal experience — anxiety, self-doubt, shame, discomfort, memories of past failure. The question is not "How do I get rid of this barrier?" but "Am I willing to have this barrier and take the action anyway?"

**Committed action plans:** A structured weekly plan that connects values to concrete behaviors:
1. Value: ___
2. Goal for this week: ___
3. Specific actions (what, when, where): ___
4. Internal barriers I expect (thoughts, feelings): ___
5. Skills I'll use to handle them (acceptance, defusion, present moment): ___
6. How I'll know I've done it: ___

**"Passengers on the bus" metaphor:** You are the driver of a bus heading in your valued direction. As you drive, unruly passengers (thoughts, feelings, memories, urges) start shouting at you: "Turn around!" "You can't go there!" "Who do you think you are?" "It's not safe!" You have a choice. You can do what the passengers demand — stop the bus, turn around, take a detour — and they'll quiet down temporarily. But you won't get where you're going. Or you can let them shout, acknowledge their presence, and keep driving toward what matters. The passengers don't actually have the power to take the wheel — they can only make noise.

**Failure as learning — workability:** In ACT, the relevant question is not "Did I succeed or fail?" but "Did this action move me toward my values or away from them?" If a committed action didn't work, it's information: what can you learn? What would you try differently? Committed action is not about perfection — it's about willingness to keep showing up, adjusting, and moving in valued directions despite setbacks.

|----------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Struggle switch | Acceptance | Illustrate how fighting pain amplifies it | When client is exhausting themselves resisting emotions |
| Quicksand | Acceptance | Show that struggle worsens the problem | When avoidance strategies are clearly backfiring |
| Chinese finger trap | Acceptance | Moving toward experience rather than away | Early introduction to acceptance concept |
| Leaves on a stream | Defusion | Practice observing thoughts without engaging | When client is fused with thought content |
| "I'm having the thought that..." | Defusion | Create distance from thoughts | Any moment of cognitive fusion |

| Chessboard | Self-as-context | You are the board, not the pieces | When client is over-identified with internal experiences |

## Psychological Flexibility in Practice

The six processes are not used in isolation — they work together as facets of psychological flexibility. A given clinical moment might call for multiple processes simultaneously:

- A client avoiding social situations might need **acceptance** (of anxiety), **defusion** (from "they'll reject me"), **values** (connection matters to me), and **committed action** (I'll go to the gathering)
- A client stuck in self-criticism might need **defusion** (from the "not good enough" story), **self-as-context** (I am more than this narrative), and **present moment** (what is actually here right now?)
- A client who has "tried everything" might need acceptance of clean discomfort, willingness to give up the struggle, and a return to values to find new direction

The goal is never symptom elimination — it is building a rich, full, meaningful life in which difficult internal experiences are carried willingly as the cost of living according to one's values.
