# psychodynamic-techniques

Psychodynamic therapy explores how unconscious processes, early relationships, and recurring patterns influence current functioning. Rooted in Freud's psychoanalysis and developed through Object Relations (Winnicott, Klein), Self Psychology (Kohut), Attachment Theory (Bowlby), and contemporary relational approaches. Evidence supports short-term psychodynamic therapy (STPP) for depression, anxiety, personality disorders, and somatization (Leichsenring & Rabung, 2008; Shedler, 2010).

## Core therapeutic techniques

### Free association

The fundamental rule: "Say whatever comes to mind without censoring — thoughts, images, memories, feelings, even if they seem irrelevant, embarrassing, or nonsensical."

**Therapist listens for:**
- Themes and recurring patterns
- Gaps, hesitations, sudden topic changes (resistance)
- Affective shifts — when emotion suddenly appears or disappears
- Connections between seemingly unrelated material
- What is not being said

**When to use:** Exploratory sessions, when structured approaches feel stuck, when client needs to process without agenda.

### Clarification

Restating or reflecting to ensure understanding and deepen exploration.

- "So when you say 'fine,' what does that actually mean for you?"
- "You mentioned feeling angry, but you smiled when you said it — what do you make of that?"
- "Let me make sure I understand — you felt X when Y happened?"

**Purpose:** Shows attunement, slows the process, invites deeper reflection.

### Confrontation (therapeutic, not aggressive)

Pointing out discrepancies, contradictions, or avoidance patterns — gently and with curiosity.

- "I notice that whenever we talk about your father, you change the subject."
- "You say you don't care about the promotion, but you've brought it up three sessions in a row."
- "You describe the relationship as perfect, but every example you give involves conflict."

**Timing:** Only after sufficient alliance. Too early = rupture. Frame as observation, not accusation.

### Interpretation

Offering a hypothesis about unconscious meaning. The most "psychodynamic" of interventions.

**Content interpretation:** What the material means.
- "It sounds like losing the job felt like losing your father's approval all over again."

**Process interpretation:** What's happening in the therapeutic relationship right now.
- "I wonder if your frustration with me right now is connected to feeling unheard by people you depend on."

**Guidelines:**
- Tentative, not declarative: "I wonder if..." / "Could it be that..."
- Well-timed: when the client is close to the insight themselves
- Dosage: one interpretation at a time, then wait
- Client's response matters more than the interpretation's "accuracy"

### Linking

Connecting present patterns to past experiences or to the therapeutic relationship.

- Past–present link: "This dynamic with your boss seems similar to what you described with your mother."
- Outside–inside link: "You feel controlled by your partner — I wonder if you sometimes feel I'm trying to control you too."
- Triangle of conflict: the impulse/feeling, the anxiety it generates, and the defense used to manage it

## Transference

Unconscious redirection of feelings, expectations, and relational patterns from significant figures onto the therapist.

### Types

| Type | Manifestation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | Idealization, attachment, trust, warmth | "You're the only one who understands me" |
| **Negative** | Hostility, mistrust, devaluation, withdrawal | "You don't really care, you're just doing your job" |
| **Erotic** | Romantic or sexual feelings toward therapist | Declarations of love, seductive behavior, fantasies |
| **Maternal/paternal** | Seeking parenting, approval, guidance | "Just tell me what to do" |

### Working with transference

1. **Notice it** — internal signals: feeling pulled to behave in a certain way, unusually strong emotions
2. **Explore it** — "What's it like being here with me right now?" / "What do you imagine I'm thinking?"
3. **Understand origins** — "Who does this remind you of?"
4. **Connect to patterns** — "Do you notice this happening in other relationships too?"
5. **Use it as data** — transference reveals the client's relational template

### Countertransference

Therapist's emotional reactions to the client. Not a flaw — essential clinical data.

**Types:**
- Concordant: feeling what the client feels (empathic identification)
- Complementary: feeling what significant others feel toward the client (e.g., feeling irritated = how the client's partner feels)
- Personal: therapist's own unresolved material activated by the client

**Management:** Self-awareness, personal therapy, supervision. Act on it clinically, never personally.

## Defense mechanisms

Unconscious psychological strategies for managing anxiety and protecting the ego. Everyone uses defenses — pathology is in rigidity, not presence.

### Primitive (immature) defenses

| Mechanism | Definition | Clinical example |
|---|---|---|
| **Denial** | Refusing to accept reality despite evidence | "I don't have a drinking problem" (despite DUI, lost job, partner leaving) |
| **Projection** | Attributing own unacceptable feelings to others | Client who is angry insists "You seem angry at me today" |
| **Splitting** | Seeing people/situations as all-good or all-bad | "My last therapist was perfect, you're terrible" (next week: reversed) |
| **Projective identification** | Projecting feelings onto another AND inducing those feelings in them | Client projects helplessness; therapist finds themselves unusually rescuing |
| **Acting out** | Expressing unconscious feelings through behavior | Missing sessions when therapy gets close to painful material |
| **Regression** | Returning to earlier developmental behavior under stress | Adult speaking in child-like voice when distressed |
| **Somatization** | Converting psychological distress into physical symptoms | Chronic headaches that appear during marital conflict |

### Neurotic defenses

| Mechanism | Definition | Clinical example |
|---|---|---|
| **Repression** | Pushing unacceptable thoughts/feelings out of awareness | "I have no memory of my childhood before age 10" |
| **Displacement** | Redirecting emotion from threatening target to safer one | Angry at boss → kicks the dog |
| **Reaction formation** | Expressing the opposite of the actual feeling | Excessive friendliness toward someone deeply resented |
| **Undoing** | Attempting to "cancel out" an unacceptable thought/action | Compulsive hand-washing after "dirty" thoughts |
| **Isolation of affect** | Separating idea from its emotional content | Describing horrific trauma in flat, clinical tone |
| **Rationalization** | Creating logical explanations for irrational behavior | "I didn't get the job because they were biased" (avoiding inadequacy feelings) |
| **Intellectualization** | Focusing on abstract/intellectual aspects to avoid emotions | Discussing grief in philosophical terms without any tears |

### Mature defenses

| Mechanism | Definition | Clinical example |
|---|---|---|
| **Sublimation** | Channeling unacceptable impulses into valued activities | Aggression channeled into competitive athletics |
| **Humor** | Expressing difficult feelings through wit | Making jokes about a painful situation (genuine humor, not avoidance) |
| **Idealization** | Attributing exaggerated positive qualities | Viewing a mentor as flawless |
| **Devaluation** | Attributing exaggerated negative qualities | "Therapy is completely useless anyway" |

### Clinical approach to defenses

- **Identify** the defense pattern
- **Understand** what it protects against (the underlying anxiety)
- **Respect** its function — it was adaptive at some point
- **Gradually explore** what lies beneath, at the client's pace
- **Never strip defenses** without something to replace them

## Object relations and attachment

### Internal working models

Mental representations of self and others formed in early relationships. They act as templates for all subsequent relationships.

- **Self-representation:** Am I worthy of love? Am I competent? Am I safe?
- **Other-representation:** Are others reliable? Responsive? Threatening? Available?

### Adult attachment styles (Bartholomew & Horowitz)

| Style | Self-model | Other-model | Relational pattern | In therapy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Secure** | Positive | Positive | Comfortable with intimacy and autonomy | Engages openly, tolerates exploration, uses therapist as secure base |
| **Anxious-preoccupied** | Negative | Positive | Fear of abandonment, clingy, needs constant reassurance | Frequent contact, distress about breaks, idealization of therapist |
| **Dismissive-avoidant** | Positive | Negative | Self-sufficient, dismisses intimacy, intellectualizes | Resists emotional depth, minimizes problems, difficulty depending on therapist |
| **Fearful-avoidant** | Negative | Negative | Desires closeness but fears it, chaotic relationships | Approach-avoidance with therapist, disorganized attachment behaviors |

### Recognizing patterns in therapy

- How does the client relate to you? (attachment style in action)
- How does the client describe key relationships? (internal working models)
- What patterns repeat? (repetition compulsion)

### Repetition compulsion

Unconsciously recreating familiar (often painful) relational dynamics. The client who was emotionally neglected chooses emotionally unavailable partners. The client who was criticized constantly criticizes themselves.

**Therapeutic approach:** Make the pattern conscious. "Do you notice a pattern here?"

## Dream analysis

### Framework

Dreams are not decoded with universal symbol dictionaries. Each client's associations are primary.

**Processing approach:**
1. "Tell me the dream in present tense, as if it's happening now."
2. "What stands out most? What's the feeling?"
3. For each element: "What comes to mind about [element]? What does it remind you of?"
4. "Does this connect to anything in your life right now?"
5. "What might this dream be trying to tell you?"

### Concepts

- **Manifest content:** The surface story of the dream
- **Latent content:** The underlying emotional meaning
- **Day residue:** Recent events that trigger dream content
- **Recurring dreams:** Often point to unresolved conflicts
- **Nightmares:** May relate to trauma, anxiety, or unexpressed feelings

## Corrective emotional experience

When the therapeutic relationship itself provides a new, reparative experience different from early relational patterns (Alexander & French, 1946).

**How it works:**
- Client expects abandonment → therapist is reliably present
- Client expects criticism → therapist is accepting
- Client expects control → therapist offers autonomy
- Client expects emotional unavailability → therapist is attuned and responsive

**Not role-playing.** Genuine therapeutic presence that naturally contradicts pathological expectations through authentic relating.

**Therapist stance:** Reliable, consistent, attuned, non-retaliatory, non-abandoning, curious, warm but boundaried.

## Common mistakes

- Interpreting too early (before alliance is established)
- Over-interpreting (seeing unconscious meaning in everything)
- Confusing confrontation with criticism
- Ignoring countertransference or acting on it personally
- Stripping defenses without providing support
- Using jargon with clients ("You're using projection right now")
- Neglecting the here-and-now therapeutic relationship
- Assuming dream symbols have universal meanings
- Forcing insight rather than letting it emerge
