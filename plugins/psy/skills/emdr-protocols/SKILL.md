# emdr-protocols

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) was developed by Francine Shapiro in 1987 and has become one of the most extensively researched treatments for PTSD and other trauma-related disorders. It is recommended as a first-line treatment for PTSD by the World Health Organization (WHO, 2013), the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, 2018), and the American Psychological Association (APA, 2017). EMDR is grounded in the Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) model, which proposes that psychological disturbance results from insufficiently processed memories — traumatic experiences are stored in state-specific form with the original perceptions, cognitions, emotions, and body sensations, unable to link with adaptive memory networks. Bilateral stimulation (BLS) during focused attention on a traumatic memory facilitates the brain's natural information processing, allowing the memory to be integrated adaptively.

**IMPORTANT:** EMDR requires specialized training and certification through an EMDR International Association (EMDRIA)-approved program. This skill provides reference material for trained EMDR practitioners. Do not attempt EMDR processing without proper training and supervision. Inadequate application can destabilize clients and cause harm.

## Standard 8-Phase Protocol

### Phase 1 — History and Treatment Planning

Comprehensive clinical history to identify processing targets and assess client readiness.

**Target identification uses a three-pronged approach:**
1. **Past events:** The earliest or most disturbing memories contributing to current symptoms. Use a timeline or floatback technique to identify touchstone memories.
2. **Current triggers:** Present-day situations, people, or stimuli that activate the maladaptive memory network.
3. **Future templates:** Desired future behaviors and responses — how the client wants to handle similar situations going forward.

**Readiness assessment:**
- Is the client sufficiently stable for trauma processing? Assess: current functioning, affect regulation capacity, dissociative symptoms, substance use, suicidality, social support.
- Stabilization first if: significant dissociative symptoms (DES > 30), active substance dependence, acute suicidality, insufficient affect tolerance, lack of safety.
- Contraindications for immediate processing: acute psychosis, unstable medical conditions (e.g., seizure disorders without medication control, pregnancy — relative contraindication requiring clinical judgment), ongoing domestic violence where processing may increase risk.

**Treatment planning:**
- Prioritize targets: typically earliest memory first, then worst, then most recent, then current triggers, then future templates.
- Estimate number of sessions needed: single-incident trauma may resolve in 3-6 sessions; complex/developmental trauma requires extended stabilization and processing, often 20+ sessions.
- Informed consent: explain the AIP model in accessible terms, describe what will happen during processing, discuss possible between-session disturbance, establish a stop signal.

### Phase 2 — Preparation

Building resources and establishing safety before any trauma processing begins. Minimum of 1-2 sessions; more for complex presentations.

**Psychoeducation:**
- Explain the AIP model: "Your brain has a natural healing system, similar to how your body heals a cut. Sometimes a traumatic memory gets 'stuck' and can't heal on its own. EMDR helps restart that healing process."
- Explain BLS: "I'll ask you to hold the memory in mind while following my fingers with your eyes [or while tapping]. This seems to help the brain process the memory."
- Normalize between-session effects: new memories, dreams, emotional shifts, physical sensations. These indicate processing is continuing.

**Resource installation — Safe/Calm Place:**
1. Guide the client to identify a real or imagined place where they feel completely safe, calm, and at peace.
2. Develop the image in detail: "What do you see? Hear? Smell? What's the temperature? What do you feel in your body?"
3. Identify a cue word (e.g., "beach," "forest," "home").
4. Add short sets of slow BLS (6-8 passes) to strengthen the positive association.
5. Test: "Bring up the image and the word — what do you notice?" Should report increased calm.
6. Practice accessing the resource between sessions.

**Containment exercise:**
- Guide the client to imagine a container (box, vault, chest) strong enough to hold difficult material.
- Practice putting distressing content "in the container" and closing it. The material is not gone — it is held safely until the next session.
- Install with BLS if desired.

**Establish a stop signal:**
- Agree on a gesture or word the client can use at any time to pause or stop processing. Emphasize that they are always in control.

### Phase 3 — Assessment

Activating the target memory network and establishing baseline measurements before processing begins.

**For each processing target, identify:**

| Component | Question | Purpose |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Image | "What picture represents the worst part of the experience?" | Activates the visual component of the memory |
| Negative Cognition (NC) | "What negative belief about yourself goes with that picture?" | Identifies the maladaptive self-referencing belief |
| Positive Cognition (PC) | "What would you rather believe about yourself?" | Identifies the desired adaptive belief |
| Validity of Cognition (VOC) | "When you think of the image, how true does [PC] feel now on a scale of 1-7?" | Baseline measure of adaptive belief strength |
| Emotions | "When you bring up the image and the words [NC], what emotions do you feel?" | Identifies the affective component |
| Subjective Units of Disturbance (SUD) | "On a scale of 0-10, how disturbing does it feel now?" | Baseline measure of disturbance |
| Body location | "Where do you feel the disturbance in your body?" | Identifies the somatic component |

**Negative Cognition categories and examples:**
- Responsibility/defectiveness: "It was my fault," "I am defective," "I should have done something," "I am shameful"
- Safety/vulnerability: "I am not safe," "I cannot trust anyone," "I am in danger," "The world is dangerous"
- Power/control: "I am powerless," "I am helpless," "I have no control," "I cannot protect myself"

**Positive Cognition requirements:**
- Must be self-referencing ("I am..." not "He was wrong to...")
- Must be believable (realistic, not grandiose)
- Must be in the present tense
- Must be the adaptive counterpart to the NC
- Examples: "I did the best I could," "I am safe now," "I have choices now," "I am worthy," "I can handle it"

**VOC scale:** 1 = completely false, 2 = mostly false, 3 = somewhat false, 4 = partly true, 5 = somewhat true, 6 = mostly true, 7 = completely true. A starting VOC of 1-2 is typical; the goal is to reach 7 by Phase 5.

**SUD scale:** 0 = no disturbance / neutral, 1-2 = slightly bothered, 3-4 = mildly distressed, 5 = moderately distressed, 6-7 = quite distressed, 8-9 = severely distressed, 10 = worst distress imaginable. The goal is to reach 0 (or 1 with ecological validity) by end of Phase 4.

### Phase 4 — Desensitization

The core processing phase. The client holds the target memory in mind while receiving BLS until disturbance resolves.

**Procedure:**
1. Instruct: "Bring up the image, the negative belief [NC], and notice where you feel it in your body. Now follow my fingers." (Or appropriate BLS modality.)
2. Deliver one set of BLS — typically 24-36 bilateral movements for eye movements, adjusting speed and duration based on client response.
3. After each set: "Let it go. Take a breath. What do you notice?" (Open-ended — do not direct.)
4. Whatever the client reports (new image, thought, emotion, sensation, memory), say: "Go with that." Begin the next set.
5. Continue until the client reports no new material and SUD = 0.

**Processing channels:** Material may shift across channels during processing:
- Images: the memory image changes, new memories emerge, the scene shifts
- Cognitions: new thoughts arise, the NC weakens, insights emerge
- Emotions: feelings intensify, shift, or resolve — e.g., fear → anger → sadness → relief
- Body sensations: tension moves, releases, or transforms
- All of these are expected and indicate adaptive processing

**When to return to target:**
- After a channel clears (client reports "nothing" or "it's calm"), return to original target: "Go back to the original memory. What do you get now?"
- Check SUD: "On a scale of 0-10, how disturbing is it now?"
- If SUD > 0 and new material arises, continue processing
- If SUD = 0, move to Phase 5

**Ecological validity check:** Sometimes SUD = 1 is appropriate if it reflects a realistic, non-pathological response ("It was a terrible thing and it's appropriate to feel some sadness about it"). The clinician uses judgment to determine if this is ecological (healthy residual emotion) vs. blocked processing.

**Incomplete session management:**
- If time runs out before SUD = 0, stop processing and move to Phase 7 (Closure).
- Do not start a new processing set within 10-15 minutes of session end.
- Document where processing stopped; resume there next session.

### Phase 5 — Installation

Strengthening the adaptive belief by pairing it with the now-desensitized memory.

**Procedure:**
1. Return to the original target memory and the PC identified in Phase 3.
2. Ask: "Do the words [PC] still fit, or is there another positive belief that fits better?" (Processing may have shifted the desired cognition.)
3. Instruct: "Hold the original event and the words [PC] together." Begin BLS.
4. After each set: "On a scale of 1-7, how true do those words feel now?"
5. Continue until VOC = 7.
6. If VOC plateaus below 7, explore what blocks full acceptance. This may indicate another memory network requiring processing.

### Phase 6 — Body Scan

Checking for and clearing any residual somatic disturbance.

**Procedure:**
1. Instruct: "Close your eyes. Hold the original memory and the positive belief [PC] together. Scan your body from head to toe. Tell me if you notice any tension, tightness, or unusual sensation."
2. If clear: target is complete. Move to Phase 7.
3. If residual sensation: process with BLS until the sensation resolves.
4. If new disturbance emerges: this may indicate a linked memory. Process if time allows; otherwise note for next session.

### Phase 7 — Closure

Every session ends with closure, whether processing is complete or incomplete.

**Complete session (SUD = 0, VOC = 7, body scan clear):**
- Debrief: "How are you feeling? Any questions?"
- Remind about possible between-session effects: "Processing may continue between sessions. You might notice new thoughts, memories, dreams, or feelings. This is normal. Keep a journal of anything that comes up."

**Incomplete session (SUD > 0):**
1. Use containment: "Let's put any remaining material in your container for now."
2. Guide the client to the safe/calm place installed in Phase 2.
3. Ensure the client is grounded and oriented to the present.
4. Brief: "We'll continue where we left off next session. Between now and then, you may notice continued processing. Use your calm place and container as needed. If anything feels overwhelming, call me."
5. Provide a journal or log sheet for between-session observations.

### Phase 8 — Reevaluation

The first task of every subsequent session.

**Procedure:**
1. Ask about between-session experiences: dreams, memories, emotions, body sensations, behavioral changes.
2. Return to the previously processed target: "Bring up the memory we worked on last time. What do you notice now?"
3. Check SUD: still 0? If not, resume processing at Phase 4.
4. Check VOC: still 7? If not, explore what shifted.
5. If the previous target is fully resolved, check current triggers. If resolved, process future template. If future template is clear, move to next target on the treatment plan.

## Bilateral Stimulation Methods

**Eye movements (most common):**
- Therapist moves fingers (or uses a light bar) horizontally in the client's visual field.
- Speed: approximately 2 full passes per second (adjust to client comfort).
- Set length: 24-36 movements. Can be shorter for fragile clients or longer during intense abreaction.
- Distance: about 13 inches from client's face, spanning the full visual field.

**Tactile (tapping):**
- Alternating taps on the client's knees or hands (therapist-administered).
- Or using handheld pulsers/buzzers that alternate vibrations between left and right hands.
- Useful for clients who: cannot sustain eye tracking, wear contact lenses, have eye conditions, or find eye movements too activating.

**Auditory:**
- Alternating tones through headphones, left-right.
- Least common but useful when other modalities are impractical.
- Can be combined with eye movements for intensified BLS.

**Self-administered BLS (stabilization only, not full processing):**
- Butterfly hug: cross arms over chest, alternately tap left and right shoulders.
- Used in Phase 2 resource installation, group EMDR, and as a self-regulation tool between sessions.
- Not a substitute for therapist-directed BLS during Phase 4 processing.

## Cognitive Interweave

A clinician-directed intervention used when adaptive processing is blocked. The therapist introduces a brief statement or question to link the stuck material with adaptive information. Returns processing to Phase 4 standard protocol immediately after.

**Types:**

**Responsibility interweave:**
- For clients stuck on self-blame. "Whose responsibility was it? If a [child/woman/person] was in that situation, would you blame them?" "How old were you? Could a child of that age be responsible?"

**Safety interweave:**
- For clients stuck in past danger. "Is it happening right now?" "Are you safe right now, in this room?" "Where are you right now — what year is it?"

**Choice/power interweave:**
- For clients stuck on helplessness. "Do you have choices now that you didn't have then?" "What can you do now that you couldn't do as a child?"

**When to use cognitive interweave:**
- **Looping:** The same material repeats across 2-3 sets without movement. The client reports the same image, thought, or sensation.
- **SUD plateau:** SUD is stuck at a level and not decreasing despite continued BLS.
- **Blocking beliefs:** An irrational belief prevents adaptive processing (e.g., "If I let go of the guilt, it means it didn't matter").
- **High abreaction:** Emotional intensity is escalating without resolution. The interweave grounds the client and introduces adaptive information.
- **Feeder memories:** Processing reveals an earlier, more foundational memory that needs to become the target.

**How to deliver:**
1. Keep it brief — one sentence or question.
2. After stating the interweave: "Go with that." Begin BLS.
3. Return to standard Phase 4 protocol immediately.
4. Use sparingly — the goal is to restart the client's own processing, not to do cognitive therapy within EMDR.

## Specialized Protocols

### Recent Traumatic Episode Protocol (R-TEP)

Developed by Elan Shapiro and Brurit Laub for events occurring within days to weeks. Useful for first responders, accident victims, and recent assault survivors.

**Procedure:**
1. **Narrative timeline:** Client tells the story of the event from "just before" to "now." Therapist identifies points of disturbance (Points of Disturbance, or PoDs).
2. **EMD (Eye Movement Desensitization):** Brief processing on each PoD, maintaining a narrow focus. Not full EMDR reprocessing — focused on desensitization.
3. Process the most disturbing PoDs with standard Phase 3-6 protocol.
4. Install future template for ongoing triggers.

### Phobia Protocol

**Target sequence:**
1. First experience of the phobic fear (when did it start?)
2. Worst experience related to the phobia
3. Most recent experience
4. Current trigger — process with standard protocol
5. Future template: imagine encountering the phobic stimulus with adaptive cognition installed

### Grief Protocol

Adapted for loss and bereavement, respecting that grief is not pathological — only stuck or complicated grief is the target.

**Procedure:**
1. Process the loss event itself (the moment of learning about the death, the funeral, etc.) if traumatic.
2. Process any unfinished business (guilt, anger, things unsaid).
3. Once trauma is resolved, install positive memories and a continuing bond with the deceased.
4. Future template: imagining the future without the person, with adaptive coping.

### Future Template

Used as the third prong of the three-pronged protocol, or standalone for performance anxiety, anticipated challenges, or goal achievement.

**Procedure:**
1. Identify the future situation.
2. Ask the client to imagine it in detail — what they will see, hear, feel.
3. Install the adaptive cognition (PC).
4. BLS to strengthen the imagined adaptive response.
5. Body scan to check for residual disturbance — any disturbance may indicate an unprocessed past memory.

### Group EMDR — IGTP and G-TEP

**IGTP (Integrative Group Treatment Protocol, Jarero et al., 2006):**
- For disaster response and mass trauma. Clients draw their trauma narrative in a workbook while receiving butterfly hug BLS.
- 8-12 participants per group.
- Does not require individual verbalization of trauma (critical for group settings and cultural sensitivity).

**G-TEP (Group Traumatic Episode Protocol, E. Shapiro, 2013):**
- Combines R-TEP with group format.
- Uses worksheets, drawing, and self-administered BLS (butterfly hug).

## Safety, Abreaction Management, and Contraindications

### Abreaction Management

Abreaction — an intense re-experiencing of traumatic affect during processing — is normal and expected. The clinician's task is to maintain dual attention (past and present simultaneously) and keep the client within the window of tolerance.

**Signs of productive abreaction:** Tears, trembling, raised voice, strong body sensations — but the client remains oriented to the present, can hear the therapist, and continues to track BLS.

**Signs of unproductive abreaction (overactivation):** Client is overwhelmed, cannot hear the therapist, freezes, dissociates, or panics.

**Management strategies:**
1. Slow down BLS speed and reduce set length.
2. Talk the client through: "You're here in my office. You're safe. That was then, this is now."
3. Increase distance from the material: "Imagine watching the memory on a TV screen" or "Imagine you're watching from far away."
4. Use cognitive interweave (safety type): "Where are you right now? Are you safe?"
5. If necessary, stop BLS and use grounding: feet on floor, 5-4-3-2-1 technique, cold water, safe place.
6. If the client cannot restabilize: close the session (Phase 7), revise the treatment plan, increase Phase 2 stabilization work.

### Containment Exercises

**Container imagery:** "Imagine a container strong enough to hold anything — a vault, a safe, a titanium box. Put the remaining distressing material inside and close it securely. The material will stay there until we open it together next session."

**Light stream technique (Shapiro):** Guide the client to notice body sensation, assign it a color or shape, then imagine a healing light entering through the top of the head, flowing to the area, and transforming the sensation.

### Contraindications and Cautions

**Absolute contraindications for EMDR processing:**
- Acute psychotic episode (active hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder)
- Active suicidality with plan and intent (stabilize safety first, then consider EMDR)
- Neurological conditions with seizure risk not controlled by medication

**Relative contraindications (proceed with caution and modification):**
- Dissociative disorders (DID, OSDD): Require extensive Phase 2 stabilization, ego-state awareness, and system cooperation before any processing. Use fractionated approaches. Never process without advanced training in dissociative disorders.
- Active substance dependence: Stabilize sobriety first. EMDR can be used for trauma underlying addiction, but with concurrent addiction treatment.
- Pregnancy: Hormonal and physiological changes may complicate processing. Clinical judgment required. Some clinicians defer processing to postpartum; others proceed with reduced intensity.
- Ongoing domestic violence: Ensure physical safety is established before trauma processing. Processing may destabilize a client who must maintain hypervigilance for survival.
- Medical instability: Recent surgery, cardiac conditions, severe chronic illness — assess risk-benefit.
- Insufficient therapeutic alliance: Processing requires deep trust. Do not rush Phase 2.

### Common Clinical Errors

- Rushing Phase 2 preparation — inadequate resources lead to destabilization during processing.
- Skipping the body scan (Phase 6) — residual somatic disturbance can re-trigger symptoms.
- Not reevaluating previous targets (Phase 8) — assumptive resolution without checking leads to incomplete treatment.
- Moving too fast for dissociative clients — titrated, fractionated processing is essential.
- Overusing cognitive interweave — it should restart the client's own processing, not replace it.
- Not addressing current triggers and future templates — resolving past memories alone does not complete treatment.
- Ignoring between-session effects — always debrief at the start of the next session.
- "Freelancing" by combining EMDR with other techniques within sets — stay with the standard protocol until proficient.
